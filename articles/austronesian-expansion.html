Austronesian expansion

Oceania · 3000 BCE – 1200 CE

Around 3000 BCE, farming communities on Taiwan began pushing outward across open water, carrying with them a language family, a suite of domesticated plants and animals, and outrigger canoe technology that would eventually span more of the globe than any other pre-industrial seafaring tradition. Their descendants moved south through the Philippines and into island Southeast Asia, then, in a series of long ocean crossings, out into the remote Pacific and across the Indian Ocean.

The Lapita culture, emerging around 1500 BCE in the Bismarck Archipelago, carried distinctive dentate-stamped pottery, pigs, chickens, and taro as far as Fiji, Tonga, and Samoa by roughly 900 BCE. From this western Polynesian base, voyagers using sophisticated wayfinding — reading stars, swells, and bird flight without instruments — settled the most isolated islands on Earth: the Marquesas and Society Islands, Hawaii by around 1000 CE, Rapa Nui (Easter Island) by roughly 1200 CE, and New Zealand at the very end of this era. In the opposite direction, Austronesian speakers crossed thousands of miles of open Indian Ocean to settle Madagascar off the coast of East Africa, a migration whose linguistic and genetic traces survive there today.

By 1200 CE, Austronesian-speaking peoples occupied a maritime realm stretching from Madagascar to Easter Island and from Taiwan to New Zealand, the widest geographic spread of any language family before the age of European colonization. Their legacy endures in the more than 1,200 Austronesian languages still spoken, in double-hulled voyaging canoe traditions revived in modern Polynesia, and in the shared crops, boatbuilding methods, and navigational knowledge that bound together societies separated by vast stretches of open ocean.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: Shang and Zhou dynasties rose and fell in China while Austronesian voyagers were settling the Philippines and Indonesia.
  • Africa: Bantu-speaking farmers were spreading across sub-Saharan Africa around the same centuries Austronesians reached Madagascar.
  • Europe: the Viking Age was opening the North Atlantic as Polynesians were completing the settlement of New Zealand and Rapa Nui.
