Indus Valley civilization

Asia · 3300 BCE – 1300 BCE

The Indus Valley civilization emerged along the Indus River and its tributaries in what is now Pakistan and northwest India, growing from early farming villages into one of the three great river-valley civilizations of the ancient world, alongside Mesopotamia and Egypt. Its mature urban phase, from roughly 2600 to 1900 BCE, produced hundreds of settlements spread across a vast area, unified by strikingly similar city planning, weights, and material culture despite the absence of any known monumental palaces or temples.

Cities such as Mohenjo-daro and Harappa were laid out on grid patterns with baked-brick houses, covered drains, and some of the earliest known urban sanitation systems, including private wells and bathrooms connected to citywide sewers. Standardized brick sizes, a uniform system of weights and measures, and widespread use of carved seals bearing animals and a still-undeciphered script point to extensive trade networks reaching Mesopotamia, the Persian Gulf, and Central Asia. The civilization left no clear evidence of kings, armies, or large-scale warfare, leading many scholars to suggest a relatively decentralized or merchant-oriented social order.

Beginning around 1900 BCE, the great cities were gradually abandoned. Likely causes include the shifting or drying of rivers such as the Ghaggar-Hakra, weakening monsoon patterns, and the disruption of the long-distance trade that had sustained urban life. Population dispersed into smaller rural settlements to the east, and the distinctive urban culture faded, though many of its technologies, crafts, and possibly linguistic and religious elements persisted into later South Asian traditions.

Because its script remains undeciphered, the Indus Valley civilization is known almost entirely through archaeology rather than texts, leaving its political structure, language, and beliefs a subject of ongoing debate. Rediscovered only in the 1920s through excavations at Harappa and Mohenjo-daro, it is now recognized as one of the largest and most sophisticated urban cultures of the Bronze Age.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Old Kingdom Egypt rose, built the Giza pyramids, and gave way to the Middle and New Kingdoms.
  • Asia: Sumerian and Akkadian city-states flourished in Mesopotamia, followed by Babylon's rise under Hammurabi.
  • Europe: Minoan Crete built its first palaces, and Stonehenge's later phases were completed in Britain.
