Independence wars

South America · 1810 – 1830

Napoleon's 1808 invasion of Spain, which toppled the Bourbon king and installed his brother on the throne, shattered the legitimacy of colonial rule across Spanish America. Local juntas formed in cities like Buenos Aires, Caracas, and Bogotá in 1810, nominally to hold power for the deposed king but quickly evolving into movements for outright independence as creole elites, long resentful of Spanish trade restrictions and exclusion from high office, seized the opening.

Two campaigns converged to break Spanish power. In the north, Simón Bolívar led armies out of Venezuela and New Granada, crossing the flooded plains and Andes in a series of daring campaigns that culminated in victories at Boyacá (1819), Carabobo (1821), and Pichincha (1822), founding the short-lived republic of Gran Colombia. In the south, José de San Martín organized an army in Argentina, marched it across the Andes into Chile, and won at Chacabuco and Maipú before sailing north to liberate Peru. The two liberators met at Guayaquil in 1822, and San Martín withdrew, leaving Bolívar and his general Antonio José de Sucre to finish the war at Ayacucho in 1824, which effectively ended Spanish rule on the mainland.

By 1830, a patchwork of new republics — Argentina, Chile, Peru, Bolivia, Colombia, Venezuela, and Ecuador among them — had replaced the viceroyalties, though Gran Colombia itself splintered that same year and Bolívar died disillusioned, having failed to hold his envisioned union together. The wars left the continent independent but fractured, saddled with weak institutions, regional caudillos, and economies still oriented toward export of raw materials, patterns that shaped Latin American politics for generations.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Napoleonic Wars ended at Waterloo in 1815, and the Congress of Vienna redrew the continent's map.
  • North America: the United States fought the War of 1812 against Britain and issued the Monroe Doctrine in 1823, partly in response to these very revolutions.
  • Asia: the Qing dynasty was tightening restrictions on foreign trade even as opium imports from British India began to surge.
