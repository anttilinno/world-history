Early Homo sapiens

Africa · 300000 BCE – 10000 BCE

Fossils from Jebel Irhoud in Morocco, dated to roughly 300,000 years ago, and from Omo Kibish and Herto in Ethiopia, show a skull shape — a rounded braincase paired with a still-projecting face — that marks the earliest known anatomically modern humans. Rather than a single "cradle," genetic and fossil evidence now points to Homo sapiens emerging from interconnected populations across Africa, gradually accumulating the skeletal traits found in living people today.

Behavioral complexity followed unevenly over the next hundred thousand years: ochre pigments and deliberately pierced shell beads at Blombos Cave in South Africa, engraved ostrich eggshells, and increasingly sophisticated stone tool traditions all point to symbolic thought and cultural transmission. Starting around 70,000–60,000 years ago, groups of Homo sapiens began pushing out of Africa in sustained waves, spreading along the Arabian coast into South Asia, reaching Australia by about 65,000 years ago, and pressing into Europe and Central Asia, where they encountered and occasionally interbred with Neanderthals and Denisovans.

By the height of the last Ice Age, roughly 20,000 years ago, Homo sapiens occupied every habitable continent except Antarctica, having crossed into the Americas via Beringia by at least 15,000 years ago. Cave art at sites like Lascaux and Altamira, bone and ivory carving, and increasingly specialized hunting technology reflect societies of growing sophistication even before agriculture.

As glaciers retreated after 10,000 BCE, warming climates set the stage for the Neolithic Revolution — the shift to farming and settled life that would soon follow in the Fertile Crescent and elsewhere. Every person alive today descends from the African populations that gave rise to this species, making the emergence of Homo sapiens the shared root of all subsequent human history.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe and western Asia: Neanderthals held the landscape for hundreds of thousands of years before disappearing around 40,000 years ago, not long after Homo sapiens arrived.
  • Asia: Denisovans occupied Siberia and parts of eastern Asia, leaving genetic traces still found in modern Melanesian and Southeast Asian populations.
  • North and South America: entirely uninhabited by humans until the very end of this period, when the first migrants crossed from Siberia and began spreading south.
