British Australia

Oceania · 1788 – 1901

In January 1788, the First Fleet of eleven ships under Captain Arthur Phillip landed at Sydney Cove, establishing a British penal colony on land that had been home to Aboriginal peoples for tens of thousands of years. Britain, having lost its American colonies as a dumping ground for convicts, turned to New South Wales, and over the following decades tens of thousands of convicts were transported there and later to Van Diemen's Land (Tasmania), Western Australia, and other outposts.

Free settlement grew alongside the penal system, and colonies at Van Diemen's Land, Western Australia, South Australia, Victoria, and Queensland were carved out and granted increasing self-government from the 1850s. The discovery of gold in Victoria and New South Wales in 1851 triggered a rush that swelled the population, funded infrastructure, and diversified society with waves of immigrants, including many from China. Pastoral expansion for sheep and wool pushed settlement deep into the interior, turning wool into the colonies' economic backbone.

This expansion came at devastating cost to Aboriginal peoples, who suffered massacres, dispossession of land, introduced disease, and the disruption of societies that had endured since the last Ice Age. Frontier violence continued for over a century as settlement pushed outward, while colonial governments offered little protection and often facilitated the seizure of traditional lands.

By the late nineteenth century, the six self-governing colonies had developed distinct identities but shared common interests in trade, defense, and immigration restriction. Constitutional conventions through the 1890s produced a plan for federation, ratified by popular referendums, and on 1 January 1901 the colonies united as the Commonwealth of Australia, a self-governing dominion within the British Empire.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the French Revolution erupted the following year, and the Napoleonic Wars would soon reshape the continent.
  • North America: the United States was ratifying its new Constitution as Australia's convict colony was founded, and would fight a Civil War before Australia federated.
  • Asia: the Qing dynasty ruled China and the Tokugawa shogunate governed Japan until Western pressure forced both open over the course of the century.
