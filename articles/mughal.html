Mughal Empire

Asia · 1526 – 1857

The Mughal Empire was founded in 1526 when Babur, a Central Asian prince descended from Timur and Genghis Khan, defeated the Delhi Sultanate at the First Battle of Panipat. Babur's grandson Akbar, who ruled from 1556 to 1605, consolidated the empire across most of the Indian subcontinent, built an efficient centralized administration, and pursued a policy of religious tolerance that integrated Hindu Rajput nobility into Mughal governance.

The empire reached its territorial and cultural peak under Akbar's successors Jahangir, Shah Jahan, and Aurangzeb. Mughal rule fused Persian, Islamic, and Indian traditions into a distinctive Persianate court culture that produced miniature painting, monumental architecture, and rich literary patronage. Shah Jahan commissioned the Taj Mahal at Agra in the 1630s and 1640s as a mausoleum for his wife Mumtaz Mahal, and it stands as the empire's most celebrated legacy, alongside the Red Fort and the Jama Masjid.

Aurangzeb's long reign (1658–1707) extended Mughal control over almost the entire subcontinent but strained the treasury with costly wars in the Deccan and provoked unrest through stricter religious policies. After his death the empire fragmented as regional governors, the Maratha Confederacy, and Sikh and Afghan powers asserted independence, while the British East India Company steadily expanded its influence through trade and conquest.

Mughal emperors retained symbolic authority in Delhi for another century, but real power had long since passed to the Company. The empire formally ended in 1857 when the British exiled the last emperor, Bahadur Shah II, after the failed Indian Rebellion, transferring rule of India directly to the British Crown the following year.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Reformation, the Thirty Years' War, and later the French Revolution and Napoleonic Wars reshaped the continent.
  • Africa: the Songhai Empire fell in the 1590s, and the Atlantic slave trade intensified along the West African coast.
  • North America: European colonization spread from Jamestown and Plymouth through the founding and expansion of the United States.
