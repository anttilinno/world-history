Song dynasty

Asia · 960 CE – 1279 CE

The Song dynasty arose in 960 CE when general Zhao Kuangyin, later Emperor Taizu, reunified much of China after the chaos of the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period. To prevent the military coups that had plagued earlier regimes, the Song built a government dominated by civil officials selected through an expanded imperial examination system, cementing scholar-bureaucrats as the backbone of the state over hereditary aristocrats or generals.

Song China became the wealthiest and most technologically advanced society of the medieval world. Its economy was semi-industrial by premodern standards: iron and steel output reached levels not matched in Europe until centuries later, and the government issued the world's first true paper currency, jiaozi, to handle a booming market economy. Movable-type printing, invented by Bi Sheng around 1040, spread literacy and cheap books; gunpowder weapons, the magnetic compass, and mechanical clockwork all matured in this era. Cities such as Kaifeng and later Hangzhou grew into the largest urban centers on Earth, supporting a vibrant merchant culture, maritime trade across the Indian Ocean, and celebrated achievements in landscape painting, poetry, and Neo-Confucian philosophy under thinkers like Zhu Xi.

Military weakness offset this prosperity. In 1127, the Jurchen Jin dynasty conquered northern China and captured the capital Kaifeng, forcing the court to flee south and establish the Southern Song at Hangzhou — a shrunken but still fabulously rich state. The Southern Song held on for another century and a half, relying on trade revenue, fortifications, and gunpowder weaponry, before the Mongols under Kublai Khan finally overran it in 1279, absorbing China into the Yuan dynasty.

Though militarily overshadowed by its steppe neighbors, the Song left a lasting legacy: its economic institutions, printing culture, and Neo-Confucian synthesis shaped Chinese civilization for the next seven centuries and influenced administrative practices across East Asia.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Normans conquered England in 1066, and Gothic cathedrals began rising as the Crusades got underway.
  • Africa: the empire of Great Zimbabwe was emerging, and the Kingdom of Mali would soon rise in the west.
  • North America: Cahokia flourished as a major mound-building city near the Mississippi, and the Ancestral Puebloans built Chaco Canyon's great houses.
