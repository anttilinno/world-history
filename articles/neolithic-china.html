Neolithic China

Asia · 7000 BCE – 1600 BCE

By around 7000 BCE, communities along the Yellow River in the north and the Yangtze River in the south had begun domesticating crops suited to their distinct environments — foxtail and broomcorn millet on the loess-soil plains of the north, and rice in the wetter Yangtze basin. These early farming villages, marked by pit-house dwellings, painted pottery, and polished stone tools, laid the agricultural foundation for all later Chinese civilization, though "China" as a political idea did not yet exist; instead dozens of regional cultures developed in parallel, exchanging techniques and styles.

Distinct archaeological cultures rose and overlapped across the millennia: Peiligang and Cishan in the north gave way to Yangshao (c. 5000–3000 BCE), known for its red-and-black painted pottery and settlements such as Banpo, while Hemudu and Majiabang cultivated rice along the lower Yangtze. In the third millennium BCE, the Longshan culture in the north and the Liangzhu culture near the Yangtze delta reached new levels of complexity, with rammed-earth walled towns, elite burials, jade workshops, and evidence of social stratification that foreshadowed state formation. Liangzhu's elaborate jade cong and bi discs, produced for ritual and status display, reflect a sophisticated craft economy supporting powerful local elites.

These late Neolithic cultures increasingly practiced small-scale bronze working, walled settlements, and organized labor, setting the stage for the semi-legendary Xia and the historically attested Shang dynasty, which emerged from this milieu by the eighteenth to sixteenth century BCE. Rather than a single cradle of civilization, Neolithic China is now understood as a mosaic of interacting regional traditions whose agricultural techniques, pottery styles, jade craftsmanship, and settlement patterns fused gradually into the literate, bronze-age states that followed.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's Nile Valley farmers were still centuries from unification under the first pharaohs.
  • Asia: Sumerian city-states rose and fell in Mesopotamia while the Indus Valley civilization flourished and collapsed.
  • Europe: farming spread from Anatolia across the continent, and megalith builders raised Stonehenge.
