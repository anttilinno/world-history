Teotihuacan

North America · 100 BCE – 550 CE

In the Basin of Mexico, some 40 kilometers northeast of modern Mexico City, a scattering of villages began coalescing around 100 BCE into a city that would soon dwarf every other settlement in the Americas. By the early centuries CE, Teotihuacan's founders — whose ethnic identity remains debated, with Nahua, Otomi, and Totonac communities all later claiming descent — had laid out the city on a rigid grid oriented to the sun, anchored by two colossal monuments: the Pyramid of the Sun and, along the great processional avenue later named the Avenue of the Dead by the Aztecs, the Pyramid of the Moon.

At its height, roughly 300–550 CE, Teotihuacan housed an estimated 100,000 to 200,000 people, making it one of the largest cities on Earth and the largest ever built in the pre-Columbian Americas. Its economy rested on intensive agriculture, controlled irrigation, and a monopoly on obsidian production, whose blades and tools were traded across Mesoamerica. The Feathered Serpent Pyramid, adorned with carved images of Tlaloc and the plumed serpent later known as Quetzalcoatl, marked the city's third great ceremonial complex and was the site of mass dedicatory sacrifices. Teotihuacan's influence radiated far beyond the valley: its distinctive architecture, pottery, and iconography appear at Maya centers such as Tikal and Copan, and inscriptions suggest the city intervened directly in Maya dynastic politics around 378 CE.

The city was organized into dense apartment compounds housing extended families and distinct ethnic enclaves, including a neighborhood associated with Oaxaca's Zapotec people — evidence of a genuinely multiethnic metropolis rather than a single tribal capital. Teotihuacan left no deciphered writing system of its own, so its rulers remain nameless and its political structure — whether a single dynasty or a collective council — is still debated by archaeologists.

Around 550 CE, the city entered a period of crisis: its major temples along the Avenue of the Dead were deliberately burned and defaced in what appears to have been targeted destruction rather than random raiding, possibly reflecting internal upheaval, drought, or the collapse of trade networks. The population dwindled sharply in the following century. Centuries later the Aztecs, who found the ruins already ancient, named the site Teotihuacan — "the place where the gods were created" — and treated it as a sacred pilgrimage destination, cementing its legendary status long after its builders had vanished from memory.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: Rome was at its imperial height and then fractured, with the Western Empire collapsing in 476 CE.
  • Asia: China cycled through the Han dynasty's rise and fall and the ensuing Period of Disunion, while the Gupta Empire flourished in India.
  • Africa: the kingdom of Aksum in the Horn of Africa was minting its own coinage and converting to Christianity.
