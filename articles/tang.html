Tang dynasty

Asia · 618 CE – 907 CE

The Tang dynasty was founded in 618 CE by Li Yuan after the collapse of the short-lived Sui dynasty, which had reunified China but exhausted itself with canal-building and failed wars. Li Yuan's son, Emperor Taizong, consolidated power and set the pattern for Tang rule: a merit-based examination system for recruiting officials, a codified legal system, and an expansionist military that pushed Tang influence deep into Central Asia along the Silk Road.

Under Taizong and his successors, including Empress Wu Zetian — China's only female emperor in her own right — the Tang reached a golden age of political stability and cultural flowering. Chang'an, the capital, grew into what was likely the largest and most cosmopolitan city on Earth, home to over a million people, with resident merchants, monks, and diplomats from Persia, India, Korea, Japan, and beyond. Buddhism, Daoism, and foreign faiths such as Nestorian Christianity and Zoroastrianism coexisted, while Tang poetry, painting, and ceramics set standards later dynasties would imitate for centuries.

The dynasty's peak under Emperor Xuanzong gave way to crisis with the An Lushan Rebellion (755–763), a devastating civil war that killed millions and permanently weakened central authority, leaving regional military governors increasingly autonomous. Though the dynasty limped on for another century and a half, plagued by eunuch power struggles, peasant uprisings, and warlordism, it never recovered its former reach, and collapsed in 907 CE, fragmenting China into the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period.

Tang China's legacy proved immense: its legal codes, bureaucratic exams, and urban models shaped Japan, Korea, and Vietnam, while its Silk Road trade networks and printed texts (including the world's oldest dated printed book, the Diamond Sutra of 868) tied China firmly into a wider Eurasian world of commerce and ideas.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Carolingian dynasty rose under Charlemagne, crowned Holy Roman Emperor in 800 CE, as Western Europe slowly rebuilt after Rome's fall.
  • Africa/Middle East: the Islamic caliphates — Umayyad, then Abbasid — expanded from Arabia across North Africa, Iberia, and the Middle East, founding Baghdad as a rival center of learning.
  • North America: Ancestral Puebloan communities and the Maya lowland cities were flourishing, even as classic Maya civilization began its slow decline.
