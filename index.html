The “center of gravity” through history

  1. 300,000 BCE — Africa (human origins)
  2. 10,000 BCE — Fertile Crescent (agriculture)
  3. 3000 BCE — Egypt & Mesopotamia
  4. 500 BCE — Mediterranean + Persia + India + China
  5. 200 CE — Rome and Han China
  6. 1200 — China (largest economy)
  7. 1500 — Europe begins oceanic expansion
  8. 1800 — Western Europe industrializes
  9. 1900 — Europe dominates globally
  10. 1945 — United States and Soviet Union
  11. 1991 — United States predominance
  12. 2020s — Multipolar world: US · China · EU · India

Surprising overlaps

  • While the Egyptian pyramids were being built (c. 2600 BCE), Stonehenge had not yet reached its final form, and Peru's Norte Chico was already building its own pyramids.
  • When Julius Caesar lived, the Han dynasty ruled China, the Maya were developing cities, and the Silk Road connected East and West.
  • While Europe experienced its so-called “Dark Ages,” Tang China was one of the world's most advanced and prosperous civilizations.
  • Under the Mongol Empire, Eurasia became more interconnected than ever — exchanging goods, ideas, technologies, and diseases.
  • When Columbus reached the Caribbean in 1492, the Ming dynasty governed China, the Aztec and Inca empires were thriving, and Songhai ruled the West African savanna.
  • During Europe's Industrial Revolution, Japan modernized at breakneck speed after the Meiji Restoration, while most of Africa fell under colonial rule.
  • Today, for the first time, every inhabited continent is linked by near-instant communication, global trade, aviation, and digital networks.
