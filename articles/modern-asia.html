Rise of modern Asia

Asia · 1945 – present

Japan emerged from defeat in World War II as the first Asian economy to industrialize on a modern scale. Under American occupation reforms and a state-guided export strategy, it rebuilt its shattered cities and by the 1960s was posting double-digit growth, becoming the world's second-largest economy by 1968. Its success in automobiles and electronics established a template — export-led manufacturing, high savings, close ties between government and industry — that neighboring economies would soon copy.

From the 1960s through the 1990s, the "Asian Tigers" — South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Singapore — followed Japan's path, moving from textiles and light assembly into shipbuilding, electronics, and semiconductors. South Korean firms like Samsung and Hyundai grew into global brands, while Singapore and Hong Kong became financial and trading hubs. Southeast Asian economies such as Malaysia, Thailand, and Indonesia rode a second wave of the same model before the 1997 Asian financial crisis exposed the risks of debt-fueled growth and forced painful restructuring.

China's transformation dwarfed all of these. Deng Xiaoping's market reforms, begun in 1978, opened the country to foreign investment and private enterprise, and over four decades China became the world's manufacturing base and, by many measures, its largest economy. India followed a different but parallel arc: economic liberalization in 1991 dismantled decades of licensing controls and unleashed growth in services, software, and pharmaceuticals, making it one of the fastest-growing major economies of the 21st century.

By the 2020s, Asia accounted for roughly half of global GDP by purchasing power, reversing a two-century-old shift toward Europe and North America. Supply chains, technology development, and financial markets now run substantially through Tokyo, Seoul, Shenzhen, Mumbai, and Singapore, restoring Asia to the economic weight it held before European industrialization — while China and India's continued rise, alongside challenges of aging populations, debt, and geopolitical rivalry, will shape the world economy for decades to come.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the continent rebuilt through the Marshall Plan and integrated economically via the Coal and Steel Community and its successor, the European Union.
  • Africa: decolonization swept the continent from the 1950s onward, followed by decades of uneven development and, more recently, a resource- and youth-driven growth wave.
  • North America: the United States anchored the postwar global order as its largest economy and currency issuer, while also becoming Asia's biggest export market and rival.
