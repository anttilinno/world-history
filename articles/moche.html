Moche

South America · 100 CE – 700 CE

The Moche culture emerged around 100 CE along the arid north coast of Peru, in river valleys such as the Moche and Chicama that cut through the coastal desert toward the Pacific. Rather than a unified empire, Moche society was likely a set of politically independent valley-states sharing a common religion, iconography, and elite culture, each ruled by warrior-priests who claimed descent from or communion with powerful deities.

Moche engineers built extensive irrigation canal systems that turned desert valleys into productive farmland, supporting dense populations and monumental construction. They raised massive adobe brick platform mounds, including the Huaca del Sol and Huaca de la Luna near modern Trujillo, and their artisans became history's most accomplished pre-Columbian metalsmiths, mastering gilding, soldering, and depletion gilding to work gold, silver, and copper alloys into ornate ceremonial regalia. Moche potters produced strikingly realistic portrait vessels and painted fineline ceramics depicting rituals, warfare, and daily life in vivid detail, giving archaeologists an unusually rich visual record of their beliefs, including elaborate sacrifice ceremonies tied to elite power.

From roughly the sixth century CE, prolonged El Niño-driven flooding and drought cycles, documented in ice-core and sediment records, strained the irrigation systems and likely destabilized Moche political structures. Southern and northern Moche polities diverged and eventually gave way to successor cultures, including the Sicán (Lambayeque) and later Chimú, who inherited much of the Moche's technical and artistic legacy on Peru's north coast.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Roman Empire reached its height and then began its long decline, splitting into eastern and western halves before Rome fell in 476 CE.
  • Asia: the Gupta Empire ushered in a golden age of Indian science and art, while China passed through the Han collapse, division, and reunification under the Sui and Tang.
  • North America: Teotihuacan dominated central Mexico as the hemisphere's largest city, and Classic Maya civilization flourished in city-states across the Yucatán and Guatemala.
