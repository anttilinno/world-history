Ancient Greece

Europe · 800 BCE – 146 BCE

After the collapse of Mycenaean palace society and a centuries-long dark age, Greek-speaking peoples reorganized from around 800 BCE into independent city-states, or poleis, scattered across the mountainous mainland, the Aegean islands, and colonies from the Black Sea to Sicily and southern Italy. Rather than uniting into one kingdom, Greece remained a patchwork of rival communities sharing a language, pantheon of gods, and pan-Hellenic festivals like the Olympic Games, first recorded in 776 BCE.

The classical period, roughly 500–323 BCE, produced the era's defining achievements. Athens pioneered democracy under reformers like Cleisthenes and Pericles, while Sparta built a militarized society around disciplined citizen-soldiers. Athenian and allied Greek forces repelled Persian invasions at Marathon and Salamis, and the resulting confidence fueled a golden age of philosophy, drama, and art: Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle laid foundations of Western philosophy; Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides, and Aristophanes shaped tragedy and comedy in Athens's open-air theatres; and the Parthenon rose atop the Acropolis. Rivalry between Athens and Sparta erupted into the ruinous Peloponnesian War (431–404 BCE), weakening the city-states and leaving them vulnerable to outside conquest.

In 338 BCE, Philip II of Macedon subdued the fractured poleis, and his son Alexander the Great went on to conquer the Persian Empire and spread Greek culture from Egypt to the borders of India, launching the Hellenistic age. Political independence never fully returned: Macedonian successor kingdoms dominated Greece until Rome, expanding eastward, defeated the Achaean League and sacked Corinth in 146 BCE, absorbing Greece into its growing empire. Yet Greek language, philosophy, science, and artistic ideals had already become the bedrock on which Roman, Byzantine, and later Western civilization would build.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Zhou dynasty gave way to China's Warring States period, while the Achaemenid Persian Empire rose and fell as Greece's great rival.
  • Africa: Egypt passed through Kushite, Persian, and Ptolemaic rule, and Carthage grew into a Mediterranean trading power.
  • South America: the Chavín culture united much of the Peruvian highlands and coast under a shared religious tradition.
