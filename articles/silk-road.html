Silk Road

Asia · 130 BCE – 1453 CE

The Silk Road was not a single road but a shifting web of overland caravan routes stretching from China through Central Asia to Persia, the Levant, and the Mediterranean. It took formal shape around 130 BCE, when the Han dynasty's envoy Zhang Qian returned from missions west with reports of Central Asian kingdoms, and Han China began actively securing trade routes through the Tarim Basin and Gansu corridor. Chinese silk moved west; horses, glass, wool, and precious stones moved east, carried by relay networks of Sogdian, Persian, and other middlemen rather than any single trader crossing the whole distance.

The network's fortunes rose and fell with the empires that policed it. It flourished when the Han, Kushan, Parthian, and Roman empires all held their ends of the route stable in the early centuries CE, and again under the Tang dynasty and the Islamic caliphates, when cities like Samarkand, Bukhara, Kashgar, and Chang'an grew wealthy as caravan hubs. Buddhism, Christianity, Manichaeism, and later Islam spread along its paths alongside goods, and technologies such as papermaking traveled from China to the Islamic world and eventually Europe. The route reached its greatest security and reach under the thirteenth- and fourteenth-century Mongol Empire, whose "Pax Mongolica" allowed travelers like Marco Polo to cross the entire span of Asia under a single, if loosely unified, political umbrella.

Overland trade declined as the Mongol khanates fragmented, plague swept the route in the 1340s, and rising regional powers made passage less secure. The fall of Constantinople to the Ottomans in 1453 cut off one of the network's key western terminals and symbolically closes the era for historians, even as some trade persisted afterward. European mariners, seeking to bypass the overland tolls and instability, soon pushed into direct sea routes to Asia, shifting the center of gravity of world trade from caravan cities to oceanic ports.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Roman Republic was expanding around the Mediterranean as the Silk Road took shape, and by its close the Byzantine Empire had just fallen to the Ottomans.
  • Africa: the kingdom of Aksum rose to become a major Red Sea trading power, linking Rome and India by sea while the Silk Road linked them by land.
  • North America: the Maya built and later abandoned their great Classic-period cities, and the Aztec Empire was rising just as the Silk Road era ended.
