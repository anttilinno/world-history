Nubia & Kush

Africa · 2500 BCE – 350 CE

South of Egypt along the Nile, the region of Nubia gave rise to a succession of African kingdoms whose fortunes were tightly bound to their northern neighbor. By around 2500 BCE, the Kingdom of Kerma had emerged as a powerful, urbanized state near the Nile's third cataract, trading gold, ivory, ebony, and slaves with Egypt while developing its own distinctive pottery and architecture. Egypt alternately traded with, raided, and colonized Nubia over the following centuries, absorbing it as a province during the New Kingdom.

As Egyptian power waned after 1100 BCE, a new Nubian kingdom centered on Napata rose to fill the vacuum, absorbing centuries of Egyptian religious and cultural influence, including worship of Amun and eventually its own tradition of pyramid-building. Around 730 BCE, the Napatan king Piye conquered a fragmented Egypt and founded the 25th Dynasty, ruling as pharaoh over a unified Nile valley stretching from the Mediterranean to deep in Africa. Kushite rule ended around 656 BCE when Assyrian invasions drove the dynasty back south, but the kingdom endured.

The Kushite capital later shifted to Meroë, further south, ushering in a long golden age known for iron smelting on an industrial scale, a still only partly deciphered Meroitic script, and hundreds of steep-sided royal pyramids at Meroë and Nuri. Meroitic Kush traded across the Red Sea and Indian Ocean world and held off a Roman incursion around 23 BCE, maintaining independence even as Egypt fell under Roman rule.

Kush's power faded gradually from internal decline, shifting trade routes, and overgrazing, and the kingdom collapsed around 350 CE after being weakened by the rising rival kingdom of Axum. Its legacy survived in Nubian culture, its pyramids, and its centuries-long demonstration that African polities beyond Egypt shaped the ancient Nile world as rulers, not merely subjects.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Minoan and later Mycenaean civilizations rose and fell in the Aegean before Kush's kings ever wore the double crown.
  • Asia: the Zhou dynasty gave way to the Warring States period, and the Han dynasty later unified China as Meroë flourished.
  • South America: the Moche and Nazca cultures were building irrigation works and monumental art on the Peruvian coast.
