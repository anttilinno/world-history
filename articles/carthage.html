Carthage

Africa · 814 BCE – 146 BCE

Carthage was founded around 814 BCE on the coast of present-day Tunisia by Phoenician settlers from Tyre, traditionally led by the queen Dido. Its position on a defensible peninsula with two natural harbors made it an ideal hub for Mediterranean trade, and it grew from a Phoenician colony into the dominant commercial power of the western Mediterranean, eclipsing its Levantine parent cities after they fell under Assyrian and later Persian control.

By the fourth and third centuries BCE, Carthage controlled a maritime empire spanning the North African coast, southern Spain, Sardinia, and western Sicily, sustained by a formidable navy, mercenary armies, and a trading network reaching as far as Britain and West Africa. Its government combined elected suffetes, a wealthy merchant oligarchy, and a senate, giving it a reputation among Greek observers as a well-ordered mixed constitution. Carthage's expansion repeatedly clashed with Greek city-states and, from 264 BCE, with Rome, triggering the three Punic Wars.

The general Hannibal Barca brought Carthage its greatest military drama, crossing the Alps with war elephants in 218 BCE and inflicting catastrophic defeats on Rome at Trebia, Lake Trasimene, and Cannae during the Second Punic War, yet ultimately failing to break Roman resolve. Rome's victory at Zama in 202 BCE stripped Carthage of its overseas territories and navy, and lingering Roman fear of a Carthaginian revival led to the Third Punic War. In 146 BCE, after a prolonged siege, Rome razed the city and enslaved or killed its population, ending Carthaginian independence.

Though the city itself was destroyed, Carthaginian commercial networks, agricultural techniques, and the Punic language left a lasting imprint on North Africa, and Rome later rebuilt Carthage as a major Roman provincial capital. Carthage's legacy endures chiefly through Roman accounts of its rivalry with Rome and through Hannibal's enduring reputation as one of history's greatest military commanders.
