Bantu expansion

Africa · 1000 BCE – 500 CE

The Bantu expansion was one of the largest population movements in human history: over roughly two millennia, farming peoples speaking related Bantu languages spread out of a homeland on the border of modern Nigeria and Cameroon to occupy most of the southern half of Africa. It was not a single migration or a conquering army but a slow, generation-by-generation dispersal of villages, carried forward by a package of skills — cultivating yams and later grains, keeping cattle and goats, and above all smelting and forging iron — that let communities settle land and grow in numbers faster than the hunter-gatherers already living there.

From around 1000 BCE the movement gathered pace along two broad fronts. One stream pushed through the equatorial rainforest of the Congo basin; another skirted its northern and eastern edges toward the Great Lakes, where ironworking and cereal farming combined into especially productive societies. From the Great Lakes region Bantu-speaking herders and farmers moved south and east, reaching the East African coast and, by roughly 300 to 500 CE, the grasslands of Southern Africa. As they advanced they absorbed, displaced, or traded with the earlier San and Batwa foragers, and their languages — today numbering some five hundred, from Swahili to Zulu — largely replaced the tongues spoken before them.

The expansion left a linguistic and cultural map that still defines sub-Saharan Africa. Iron tools and weapons, mixed farming, and dense settled populations were the foundation on which later states — the Great Zimbabwe stone cities, the kingdoms of the Congo and the Great Lakes, the Swahili trading towns — would eventually be built. Because the movement was gradual and left no written record, its story has been reconstructed largely from historical linguistics, archaeology, and more recently genetics, all of which trace the same fan of related peoples spreading south and east from a single West African cradle.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: Rome grew from a small republic into a Mediterranean empire over these same centuries, and had begun to fragment by the time the expansion reached Southern Africa.
  • Asia: the Han dynasty unified China and opened the Silk Road, linking East Asia to the Mediterranean by land.
  • North America: the Olmec and then the Maya built the first cities and writing systems of Mesoamerica.
