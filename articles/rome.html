Ancient Rome

Europe · 509 BCE – 476 CE

Tradition holds that Rome was founded in 753 BCE, but its lasting political shape began in 509 BCE, when the last king was expelled and the city became a republic governed by elected magistrates, a senate of aristocrats, and popular assemblies. Over the following centuries this small city-state on the Tiber conquered first Italy, then rivals such as Carthage in the Punic Wars, and finally the entire Mediterranean basin, absorbing Greek culture and administrative practices along the way.

The republic's success bred instability: conquest concentrated wealth and armies in the hands of ambitious generals, and civil wars eventually toppled the system. Julius Caesar's rise and assassination in 44 BCE gave way to his adopted heir Octavian, who in 27 BCE became Augustus, the first emperor. Under the Pax Romana that followed, Rome ruled some 60 million people across three continents, linked by a network of roads, aqueducts, and a body of law that still underlies much of Western legal tradition. Latin spread as the language of administration and later evolved into the Romance languages.

By the 3rd century CE, plague, invasion, and economic strain forced repeated reorganization; Diocletian split the empire into eastern and western halves, and Constantine legalized and later favored Christianity, moving the capital east to Constantinople. The Western Roman Empire, weakened by decades of pressure from Germanic peoples, finally ended in 476 CE when the Germanic general Odoacer deposed the last emperor, Romulus Augustulus. The Eastern half survived for another millennium as the Byzantine Empire, carrying Roman law, institutions, and identity forward.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Han dynasty ruled a comparably vast empire in China, trading with Rome indirectly along the Silk Road.
  • Africa: the kingdom of Aksum rose in the Horn of Africa, minting its own coinage and adopting Christianity.
  • North America: the Maya were building early cities and developing the calendar and writing systems of their classic civilization.
