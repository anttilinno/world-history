Colonial Africa

Africa · 1880 – 1960

Before 1880, European footholds in Africa were mostly coastal trading posts, but rapid industrial growth, national rivalry, and new technologies — quinine to counter malaria, steamships, and the Maxim gun — drew European powers into the continent's interior. The 1884–85 Berlin Conference, hosted by Otto von Bismarck, set rules for European claims without African representation, formalizing what came to be called the Scramble for Africa. Within roughly two decades, Britain, France, Germany, Belgium, Portugal, Italy, and Spain had partitioned nearly the entire continent, leaving only Ethiopia and Liberia outside direct colonial rule.

Colonial regimes varied in form but shared an extractive logic: cash-crop agriculture, mineral extraction, forced and migrant labor, and railways built to move resources to the coast rather than connect African regions to one another. King Leopold II's personal rule over the Congo Free State became notorious for brutal rubber quotas and mass killings, prompting international outcry and Belgian state takeover in 1908. Britain and France governed through a mix of direct administration and "indirect rule" via local chiefs, while settler colonies such as Kenya, Rhodesia, and Algeria saw large-scale European land seizure. Christian missions spread alongside colonial administration, reshaping education and religion across the continent.

Africans resisted colonial rule from the outset, through armed uprisings like the Herero and Nama genocide in German South-West Africa and the Maji Maji rebellion in German East Africa, and later through organized nationalist movements. Two world wars, in which African soldiers and resources were mobilized for European causes, eroded colonial legitimacy and exposed contradictions between rhetoric of freedom and colonial subjugation. A wave of independence movements gathered pace after 1945, led by figures such as Kwame Nkrumah in Ghana and Gamal Abdel Nasser in Egypt.

Ghana's 1957 independence under Nkrumah opened the floodgates: 1960 alone saw seventeen African nations gain sovereignty, a moment remembered as the "Year of Africa." Decolonization continued unevenly into the following decades, but the borders, infrastructure, and economic patterns imposed during the colonial era left lasting legacies — including artificial state boundaries that ignored ethnic and linguistic divisions, and dependent economies still felt across the continent today.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: two world wars reshaped the continent's borders, economies, and empires, ending with the Cold War division of East and West.
  • Asia: Japan industrialized and expanded, China endured revolution and civil war, and India won independence from Britain in 1947.
  • North America: the United States rose to global superpower status through both world wars and into the atomic age.
