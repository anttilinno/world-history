Independent Africa

Africa · 1960 – present

By the late 1950s, decades of nationalist agitation, wartime service, and shifting global opinion had made colonial rule increasingly untenable. Ghana led the way to independence in 1957 under Kwame Nkrumah, but 1960 became the watershed: seventeen African nations, most of them former French colonies, gained sovereignty in a single year that came to be called the "Year of Africa." Over the following decade and a half, Britain, France, Belgium, and eventually Portugal (after prolonged liberation wars in Angola, Mozambique, and Guinea-Bissau) withdrew from the continent, and the Organisation of African Unity, founded in 1963, enshrined the colonial-era borders as the basis for the new states.

Independence did not bring uniform stability. Many young states inherited economies built for extraction rather than development, arbitrary borders that split ethnic groups, and thin institutional foundations. Coups, one-party rule, Cold War proxy conflicts (in Congo, Angola, and the Horn of Africa), and civil wars marked much of the 1960s–1990s, even as leaders like Julius Nyerere, Kenneth Kaunda, and Nelson Mandela — whose 1994 election ended apartheid in South Africa, the continent's last major liberation struggle — offered contrasting visions of nation-building. Economic liberalization, debt crises, and structural adjustment reshaped many economies in the 1980s and 1990s.

Since around 2000, much of the continent has seen sustained economic growth, expanding democratic practice, and dramatic gains in mobile telecommunications and connectivity, alongside persistent challenges of governance, conflict in the Sahel and parts of Central Africa, and climate vulnerability. Africa today has the world's youngest population by a wide margin and its fastest rate of urbanization, with megacities like Lagos, Kinshasa, and Nairobi projected to be among the largest in the world by mid-century. The African Continental Free Trade Area, launched in 2018, signals an ambition toward deeper economic integration.

The story since 1960 is thus one of both fragility and remarkable dynamism: fifty-four sovereign nations forging identities, institutions, and economies out of colonial inheritance, now shaping a demographic future that will make Africa central to the twenty-first century's global story.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: China and India pursued divergent paths of development while much of Southeast Asia also decolonized and industrialized.
  • Europe: the Cold War split the continent, then the Soviet bloc collapsed in 1989–91 and the European Union expanded eastward.
  • North America: the US moved through civil rights, the Cold War, and the digital revolution while its companies and capital became deeply entangled in Africa's postcolonial economies.
