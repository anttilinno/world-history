Neanderthals

Europe · 300000 BCE – 40000 BCE

Neanderthals (Homo neanderthalensis) evolved in Europe and western Asia from populations descended from an earlier migration of Homo heidelbergensis, with recognizably Neanderthal skeletal traits appearing by around 300,000 years ago. Over hundreds of thousands of years they adapted physically and culturally to the cold, fluctuating climates of Ice Age Eurasia, developing stocky, muscular bodies suited to conserving heat and enduring the harsh terrain of glacial and interglacial cycles alike.

Neanderthals were capable hunters and toolmakers, producing the flake-based Mousterian stone tool industry, controlling fire, crafting simple clothing and shelters, and hunting large Ice Age mammals such as mammoths, woolly rhinoceroses, and reindeer. Evidence increasingly points to symbolic behavior — deliberate burial of the dead, use of pigments, and possibly cave markings and personal ornaments — suggesting cognitive lives more complex than the "brutish caveman" stereotype long attached to them.

Neanderthal range stretched from the Iberian Peninsula and Britain through Central Europe into the Levant, Central Asia, and Siberia, where they overlapped with Denisovans. When anatomically modern humans expanded out of Africa and into Europe and Asia beginning around 60,000–50,000 years ago, the two populations coexisted and interbred, leaving most non-African humans today with roughly 1–2% Neanderthal DNA.

Neanderthals vanished as a distinct population by around 40,000 years ago, with the last known groups surviving in refugia such as Gibraltar. Their extinction likely resulted from a combination of factors — competition with incoming Homo sapiens, small and fragmented populations, climate volatility, and interbreeding that absorbed some lineages into the expanding modern human gene pool — rather than a single cause.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Homo sapiens emerged and diversified, with early anatomically modern humans (e.g. at Jebel Irhoud) present by roughly 300,000 years ago.
  • Asia: Denisovans occupied Siberia and parts of East and Southeast Asia, while Homo erectus persisted in Java into this same span.
  • Oceania: humans had not yet arrived; the first crossings into Australia occurred only near the very end of the Neanderthals' existence, around 65,000 years ago.
