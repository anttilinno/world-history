Gupta Empire

Asia · 320 CE – 550 CE

The Gupta Empire was founded around 320 CE by Chandragupta I, who built a power base in the Magadha region of northern India along the Ganges — the same heartland that had earlier produced the Mauryan Empire. Through conquest and strategic marriage alliances, his successors Samudragupta and Chandragupta II expanded Gupta control across most of the Indian subcontinent, reviving a large, prosperous, centralized state after centuries of political fragmentation.

Under Chandragupta II (reigned c. 375–415 CE), often called the golden age of the empire, the Gupta court at Pataliputra became a center of extraordinary intellectual and artistic achievement. Indian mathematicians and astronomers, including Aryabhata, developed the decimal place-value system and the concept of zero as a number, innovations that would eventually reshape mathematics worldwide. Sanskrit literature flourished, most famously in the plays and poetry of Kalidasa, while advances came in medicine, metallurgy — exemplified by the rust-resistant iron pillar of Delhi — and astronomy. Hindu temple architecture, Buddhist learning at centers like Nalanda, and rich sculptural traditions all matured during this era, giving it a lasting reputation as India's classical age.

Gupta prosperity rested on extensive trade networks, agricultural wealth, and a relatively decentralized administration that left considerable autonomy to local rulers and guilds. From the mid-5th century, invasions by the Huna (Hephthalite, or "White Hun") peoples from Central Asia repeatedly battered the empire's northwestern territories, draining its treasury and undermining central authority. Combined with the loss of provinces to regional dynasties, these pressures fragmented Gupta power, and by around 550 CE the empire had dissolved into a patchwork of smaller successor kingdoms — though its cultural and scientific legacy continued to shape Indian civilization for centuries afterward.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Western Roman Empire was crumbling under barbarian pressure, falling in 476 CE.
  • Africa: the Kingdom of Aksum in the Horn of Africa was a major trading power minting its own coinage.
  • Asia: China's Jin dynasty fractured, ushering in the turbulent Northern and Southern Dynasties period.
