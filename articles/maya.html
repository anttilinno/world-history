Maya civilization

North America · 1000 BCE – 1524

The Maya emerged in the Preclassic period, around 1000 BCE, among farming villages in the lowlands and highlands of what is now southern Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, and parts of Honduras and El Salvador. Rather than a single unified empire, the Maya developed as a network of independent city-states sharing a common language family, religion, and calendar, connected by trade in obsidian, jade, cacao, and salt. Early centers such as El Mirador grew into monumental cities with towering pyramids well before the Classic period began.

The Classic period (c. 250–900 CE) marked the civilization's height, with rival city-states like Tikal, Calakmul, Copán, and Palenque competing for dominance through warfare and alliance while producing the era's greatest achievements. The Maya devised the only fully developed writing system in the pre-Columbian Americas, carved into stone monuments and painted on bark-paper books, and their astronomers tracked the movements of Venus and the sun with remarkable precision. Maya mathematicians used a vigesimal (base-20) positional number system that included an independent invention of the concept of zero, enabling the complex calendrical calculations behind the Long Count calendar.

Between the 8th and 10th centuries, most of the great southern lowland cities collapsed in the face of drought, warfare, overpopulation, and political fragmentation, though scholars still debate the exact mix of causes. Maya civilization did not vanish, however: northern Yucatán cities such as Chichén Itzá and later Mayapán flourished for centuries afterward, and independent Maya kingdoms persisted until the Spanish conquest, with the last independent city, Nojpetén, falling only in 1697. Millions of Maya people and dozens of Mayan languages endure today across the region.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: as Classic Maya cities flourished, Rome rose and fell, and medieval kingdoms took shape in its wake.
  • Asia: Tang dynasty China and the Abbasid Caliphate presided over golden ages of trade and scholarship.
  • Africa: the kingdom of Aksum thrived and gave way to the rise of the Mali and Songhai empires.
