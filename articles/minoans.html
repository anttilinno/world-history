Minoans

Europe · 2700 BCE – 1450 BCE

From around 2700 BCE, Bronze Age communities on Crete grew into a sophisticated maritime society later named "Minoan" by archaeologist Arthur Evans, after the legendary King Minos. Drawing on trade links with Egypt, the Levant, and the Cyclades, the Cretans developed metalworking, seafaring, and eventually a system of writing, laying the groundwork for Europe's first advanced civilization.

The Minoans' hallmark achievement was the palace: sprawling, unwalled complexes at Knossos, Phaistos, Malia, and Zakros, first built around 1900 BCE and rebuilt on a grander scale after 1700 BCE. These labyrinthine structures combined royal residences, storerooms, workshops, and shrines, decorated with vivid frescoes of bull-leapers, dolphins, and processions. Minoan scribes kept administrative records first in a still-undeciphered script called Linear A, and Crete's ships and trade networks made it the dominant power of the Aegean, exporting fine pottery and olive oil across the eastern Mediterranean.

The civilization's peak, sometimes called the Neopalatial period (c. 1700–1450 BCE), ended amid a cluster of disasters: the massive volcanic eruption of Thera (Santorini) around 1600 BCE, earthquakes, and possible internal unrest all weakened Cretan society. By about 1450 BCE, most palaces outside Knossos lay in ruins, and Mycenaean Greeks from the mainland took control of the island, adapting Minoan administration into the Greek-language Linear B script.

Though political power passed to the Mycenaeans, Minoan art, religion, and technology left a deep imprint on later Greek culture, and myths of Minos, the Minotaur, and the labyrinth preserved a distorted memory of Crete's palatial age into classical antiquity.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's Old, Middle, and early New Kingdoms rose and fell, from the pyramid builders to Hatshepsut's reign.
  • Asia: the Indus Valley civilization flourished and declined, while Babylon rose under Hammurabi in Mesopotamia.
  • South America: Norte Chico's coastal cities in Peru were building some of the Americas' earliest monumental architecture.
