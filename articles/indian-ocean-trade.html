Indian Ocean monsoon trade

Africa – Asia · 100 BCE – 1500 CE

Long before the age of European sail, the Indian Ocean was the world's busiest maritime highway, and its engine was the monsoon. Twice a year the winds reverse — blowing steadily from the southwest in summer and the northeast in winter — so a captain who knew the calendar could ride the weather from Africa to India and wait for the season to turn before sailing home. This predictability knit together a vast seasonal network linking the East African coast, Arabia, Persia, the Indian subcontinent, Sri Lanka, and Southeast Asia, with China anchoring its eastern end. A first-century Greek merchant's handbook, the Periplus of the Erythraean Sea, already describes the ports, goods, and sailing times of a system in full swing.

Along these routes moved spices from the Indies, cotton textiles from Gujarat, gold and ivory from the African interior, incense from Arabia, and porcelain and silk from China, exchanged at cosmopolitan port cities where Arab, Persian, Indian, Malay, and Swahili merchants mingled. The rise of Islam from the seventh century gave the network a shared commercial law, language, and faith that lubricated trade across its whole span, and the sturdy lateen-rigged dhow carried both cargo and culture. The Swahili city-states of Kilwa and Mombasa, the spice port of Calicut, and the Malacca Strait grew rich as hinges of this commerce, and in the early fifteenth century the enormous fleets of the Chinese admiral Zheng He sailed its length as far as Africa.

What ended the old order was not decline but conquest from outside. When Vasco da Gama rounded the Cape of Good Hope and reached Calicut in 1498, the Portuguese entered an ocean whose trade had long been open and largely peaceful, and set about seizing its choke points by force. Around 1500 the balance began to tip toward armed European trading empires that taxed, monopolized, and militarized routes that had run on the monsoon and mutual profit for fifteen centuries — the beginning of the ocean's absorption into a globalized, and increasingly colonial, world economy.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: the kingdom of Aksum and, later, the Swahili city-states grew wealthy as the network's western terminals, trading gold and ivory for Asian goods.
  • Europe: from Rome through the medieval centuries to the eve of the Renaissance, Europeans knew Eastern spices only as costly imports carried the last leg overland.
  • Americas: the Maya, and later the Aztec and Inca empires, built great states entirely outside this Old World web of exchange.
