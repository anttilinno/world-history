Ottoman Empire

Asia · 1299 – 1922

The Ottoman state began around 1299 as a small Turkish beylik in northwestern Anatolia, founded by Osman I amid the collapse of Byzantine and Seljuk authority. Over the following century his successors expanded steadily into the Balkans and across Anatolia, building a disciplined military built partly around the Janissary corps, elite infantry recruited through the devshirme levy of Christian boys.

The empire's defining moment came in 1453, when Mehmed II's forces breached the ancient walls of Constantinople, ending the Byzantine Empire and giving the Ottomans a capital straddling Europe and Asia. Under Selim I and Suleiman the Magnificent in the 16th century, Ottoman power reached its height: Egypt, the Levant, the Hejaz, Iraq, and much of North Africa were absorbed, while Ottoman armies pushed deep into Hungary and twice besieged Vienna. Suleiman's reign also brought a golden age of law, architecture, and administration, with the empire governing a vast, multi-ethnic, multi-religious population under a single sultan-caliph.

From the late 16th century the empire faced slower growth, fiscal strain, and mounting military pressure from Russia and the Habsburgs, though it remained a major power for centuries and undertook repeated waves of reform, notably the 19th-century Tanzimat modernization of law, education, and administration. Territorial losses accelerated through the 1800s and early 1900s as nationalist movements fractured the Balkans and European rivals encroached.

Defeat as a Central Power in World War I proved fatal: occupation, the Turkish War of Independence, and the abolition of the sultanate ended the empire in 1922, with the Republic of Turkey proclaimed the following year as its successor state. At its peak the Ottoman Empire had governed southeastern Europe, western Asia, and North Africa for over six centuries, leaving lasting legal, architectural, and cultural imprints across three continents.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Renaissance, Reformation, and rise of Atlantic maritime empires unfolded as the Ottomans dominated the eastern Mediterranean.
  • North America: Indigenous nations gave way to European colonization, and the United States rose and expanded during the empire's last centuries.
  • Asia: Ming and Qing China, Mughal India, and Safavid then Qajar Persia rose and fell alongside the Ottoman state.
