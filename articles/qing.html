Qing dynasty

Asia · 1644 – 1912

In 1644, the Manchu people of the northeast, led by the Aisin Gioro clan, swept through a weakened Ming empire and, with the help of defecting Ming general Wu Sangui, took Beijing. Over the following decades they extended control across China proper, absorbing Ming loyalist resistance and eventually incorporating Taiwan, Mongolia, Tibet, and Xinjiang, roughly doubling the territory of the preceding dynasty and creating a vast multiethnic empire ruled by a small Manchu elite over an overwhelmingly Han Chinese population.

The reigns of the Kangxi, Yongzheng, and Qianlong emperors, spanning most of the 18th century, marked the dynasty's high point: population and agricultural output roughly tripled, the empire enjoyed relative internal peace, and Qing armies pushed its borders to their greatest extent. The court patronized Confucian scholarship, compiled massive literary encyclopedias, and presided over a sophisticated bureaucracy staffed through civil service examinations, while cautiously limiting contact with European traders to a handful of southern ports.

The 19th century brought sustained crisis. Defeat in the Opium Wars (1839–1842, 1856–1860) forced China to open treaty ports, cede Hong Kong, and grant foreign powers extraterritorial privileges, while the Taiping Rebellion (1850–1864) killed tens of millions and nearly toppled the state. Further humiliation followed in the Sino-Japanese War of 1894–1895, which cost China Taiwan and influence over Korea, and the Boxer Rebellion of 1900, which brought foreign armies into Beijing itself. Belated reform efforts could not keep pace with internal unrest and foreign encroachment.

The Xinhai Revolution of 1911 spread rapidly across provinces demanding an end to Manchu rule, and in February 1912 the child emperor Puyi abdicated, ending both the Qing dynasty and more than two thousand years of imperial rule in China. Its collapse gave way to the fragile Republic of China and decades of warlordism, but the territorial extent the Qing achieved still largely defines the borders of modern China.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Qing era spanned the Enlightenment, the French Revolution, the Napoleonic Wars, and the Industrial Revolution that gave Western powers the technological edge to humble China militarily.
  • North America: the Thirteen Colonies declared independence, fought a civil war, and industrialized into a continental power within the Qing's lifespan.
  • Africa: the "Scramble for Africa" carved most of the continent into European colonies during the same decades the Qing were losing ground to foreign imperialism at home.
