Mississippian culture & Cahokia

North America · 800 CE – 1540 CE

Around 800 CE, societies across the river valleys of the American Midwest and Southeast began shifting toward intensive maize agriculture, larger settlements, and the construction of massive earthen mounds. This Mississippian culture spread along the Mississippi, Ohio, and Tennessee river systems, linking hundreds of communities through shared religious symbolism, trade networks, and a chiefdom-based political structure centered on platform mounds topped with temples and elite residences.

The culture's greatest expression was Cahokia, near present-day St. Louis, which grew rapidly after about 1050 CE into a city of an estimated 10,000 to 20,000 people — larger than London at the time. Cahokia's skyline was dominated by Monks Mound, a ten-story earthwork covering some 14 acres, surrounded by plazas, smaller mounds, and a massive timber circle nicknamed "Woodhenge" that tracked solstices and equinoxes. Cahokia anchored a trade network reaching the Gulf of Mexico and the Great Lakes, exchanging shell, copper, and mica, while its artisans produced the elaborate iconography of the so-called Southeastern Ceremonial Complex.

Cahokia's population declined sharply after 1200 CE, likely from a combination of resource depletion, flooding, deforestation, and social strain, and the site was largely abandoned by 1350. Other Mississippian centers, such as Moundville in Alabama and Etowah in Georgia, persisted for generations afterward. When Spanish expeditions under Hernando de Soto marched through the Southeast in the 1540s, they encountered still-thriving mound-building chiefdoms, but the diseases and disruption that followed European contact hastened the collapse of the Mississippian world, whose descendant peoples include the Muscogee, Choctaw, and Natchez nations.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Carolingian Empire was fragmenting as Vikings raided the coasts, while centuries later Cahokia's decline coincided with the Black Death sweeping Europe.
  • Asia: Tang and later Song China flourished with printing and gunpowder, and the Mongol Empire rose and spanned the continent.
  • Africa: the Swahili coast city-states and the Mali Empire, home to Timbuktu and Mansa Musa, grew wealthy on gold and trans-Saharan trade.
