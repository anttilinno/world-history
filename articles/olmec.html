Olmecs

North America · 1500 BCE – 400 BCE

By around 1500 BCE, communities in the humid lowlands of Mexico's Gulf Coast, in what are now the states of Veracruz and Tabasco, had begun consolidating into the complex society historians call the Olmec — a name borrowed from a much later Nahuatl word meaning "rubber people," since the Olmecs themselves left no name for their culture in a form that survives. Rich river-valley agriculture, especially maize, supported growing populations and a class of rulers and priests who organized labor on a scale unseen before in Mesoamerica.

The Olmec heartland's major centers — San Lorenzo (dominant c. 1200–900 BCE), then La Venta (c. 900–400 BCE), and later Tres Zapotes — produced the first monumental architecture and sculpture in the Americas. Most famous are the colossal stone heads: multi-ton basalt boulders, quarried in the Tuxtla Mountains and hauled dozens of miles without wheeled transport or draft animals, then carved into portraits of individual rulers wearing tight-fitting helmets. Olmec artisans also carved jade and greenstone figurines, established long-distance trade networks reaching into the Mexican highlands and Central America, and developed early forms of the calendar, writing, and the Mesoamerican ballgame that would echo through later cultures.

Because so many hallmarks of later Mesoamerican civilization — pyramid platforms, jaguar and feathered-serpent imagery, hieroglyphic writing, the ballgame, the Long Count calendar's antecedents — appear first in Olmec contexts, archaeologists often call the Olmec the region's "mother culture," though scholars debate how directly Olmec traits were inherited versus independently developed by neighboring peoples. La Venta was abandoned around 400 BCE amid unclear causes, possibly environmental change or shifting trade routes, and Olmec influence gave way to successor cultures such as the Zapotec and the early Maya, who carried Olmec artistic and religious ideas forward across Mesoamerica.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's New Kingdom pharaohs, including Ramesses II, ruled at the height of Egyptian power.
  • Asia: China's Shang and early Zhou dynasties were casting ornate bronze vessels along the Yellow River.
  • Europe: Mycenaean Greece flourished and then collapsed, ushering in the Greek Dark Ages.
