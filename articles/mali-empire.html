Mali Empire

Africa · 1235 – 1600

The Mali Empire emerged from the West African savanna after Sundiata Keita, a Malinke prince, defeated the rival ruler Soumaoro Kante at the Battle of Kirina around 1235. Sundiata unified the small Mande-speaking chiefdoms of the upper Niger River into a single state, laying the political and legal foundations later celebrated in the Kurukan Fuga charter, an oral constitution regulating clans, resources, and succession.

Mali grew rich by controlling the trans-Saharan trade routes that carried gold from the forests to the south and salt from the Saharan mines of Taghaza to the north, taxing every caravan that crossed its territory. The empire reached its height under Mansa Musa (r. 1312–1337), whose 1324 pilgrimage to Mecca, accompanied by thousands of attendants and enormous quantities of gold, became legendary and put Mali on European maps as one of the wealthiest realms on earth. Under his patronage, Timbuktu and Djenne flourished as centers of Islamic scholarship, trade, and architecture, home to the Sankore mosque-university and vast libraries of manuscripts.

After Mansa Musa's death, Mali's territory and cohesion gradually weakened through succession disputes, pressure from the rising Songhai Empire to the east, and the secession of tributary states such as Timbuktu itself, which fell to Songhai in 1468. Tuareg raids, Mossi incursions, and Portuguese contact along the Atlantic coast further eroded Malian authority.

By the late 1500s the empire had shrunk to a small remnant state around its Manding heartland before disappearing as a political power, though its legacy persisted: Mali's wealth, Islamic learning, and imperial model shaped West African statecraft for centuries and its memory endures as a symbol of African prosperity and scholarship.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Yuan dynasty gave way to the Ming in China, and the Ottoman Empire was rising in Anatolia.
  • North America: the Aztec Empire was being founded and expanding across the Valley of Mexico.
