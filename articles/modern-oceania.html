Modern Oceania

Oceania · 1901 – present

The 20th century opened with Oceania's British colonies moving toward self-government: the Australian colonies federated into the Commonwealth of Australia in 1901, and New Zealand became a dominion in 1907. Both sent volunteer forces to fight for Britain in the First World War, and the landings at Gallipoli in 1915 forged the ANZAC tradition — a shared memory of sacrifice that became central to national identity in both countries. Meanwhile Pacific Islanders across Melanesia, Polynesia, and Micronesia remained under colonial administration by Britain, France, Germany, the United States, and later Japan and Australia.

The Second World War brought fighting directly to the region for the first time: Japan's advance through Southeast Asia and the Pacific reached New Guinea and threatened Australia itself, prompting a decisive shift in Canberra's strategic alignment from Britain toward the United States. Australian and New Zealand troops fought again alongside American forces in the Pacific campaigns, and after 1945 both countries formalized their new alliance through ANZUS (1951) and later commitments in Korea and Vietnam.

From the 1960s onward, decolonization reshaped the Pacific: Western Samoa gained independence in 1962, followed by Fiji, Tonga, Papua New Guinea, the Solomon Islands, Vanuatu, and Kiribati, among others, over the following decades. Australia and New Zealand evolved into multicultural, increasingly independent-minded democracies, ending the White Australia policy, extending recognition to Indigenous and Māori rights, and adopting more assertive regional foreign policies.

Today the defining challenge for Oceania is climate change: rising seas threaten the very existence of low-lying atoll nations such as Tuvalu and Kiribati, driving small island states to become some of the world's most vocal advocates for emissions cuts and climate justice, while larger powers compete for influence across a Pacific reshaped by both environmental peril and renewed geopolitical rivalry.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: two world wars and the Cold War redrew the continent's borders and alliances.
  • Asia: Japan's rise and fall, decolonization, and China's transformation reshaped the region.
  • Africa: waves of independence swept the continent from the 1950s through the 1970s.
