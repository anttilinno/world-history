Byzantine Empire

Europe · 330 CE – 1453 CE

In 330 CE, Emperor Constantine I dedicated a new capital, Constantinople, on the site of the Greek city of Byzantium, refounding the eastern Roman Empire around a city that would remain its heart for over a millennium. When the western Roman Empire fragmented and fell in the 5th century, the eastern half endured, retaining Roman law, imperial administration, and Christianity while its population and culture grew increasingly Greek-speaking.

Under Justinian I in the 6th century, the empire reached a first peak, reconquering North Africa and Italy, codifying Roman law in the Corpus Juris Civilis, and building the Hagia Sophia. Later centuries brought both crisis and revival: the loss of Egypt, Syria, and North Africa to the Arab conquests was followed by a resurgence under the Macedonian dynasty (867–1056), when Byzantium projected power across the eastern Mediterranean, spread Orthodox Christianity to the Slavs, and sustained a sophisticated bureaucracy, economy, and artistic tradition.

The empire's fortunes turned after the disastrous defeat at Manzikert in 1071 and catastrophic damage from the Fourth Crusade, when Latin crusaders sacked Constantinople in 1204 and carved up its territory. A reduced Byzantine state limped on for two more centuries, besieged by Ottoman Turks, Serbs, and Italian maritime republics, until Sultan Mehmed II's forces breached Constantinople's walls on 29 May 1453, ending the last direct continuation of the Roman Empire.

Byzantium's legacy proved vast: it preserved Greek and Roman learning that fed into the Renaissance, shaped Orthodox Christianity across Eastern Europe and Russia, and left behind an artistic and architectural tradition, from mosaics to domed churches, that influenced builders from Venice to Moscow.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Tang, Song, and Ming dynasties rose and fell in China while the Byzantines held Constantinople.
  • Africa: the Kingdom of Aksum gave way to Ethiopia's Solomonic dynasty, and the Mali and Songhai empires flourished in West Africa.
  • North America: the Mississippian culture built Cahokia, and the Aztec Empire rose in central Mexico just as Byzantium was falling.
