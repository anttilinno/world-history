Ghana Empire

Africa · 300 CE – 1235 CE

The Ghana Empire, known to its own people as Wagadou, emerged among the Soninke people in the region between the Senegal and Niger rivers, in what is now southeastern Mauritania and western Mali. Its rise from around the 3rd century CE was rooted in control of the trans-Saharan trade routes that linked the gold fields of West Africa to the salt mines of the Sahara and, beyond them, to North Africa and the Mediterranean world. Arab and Berber geographers, who first wrote of the kingdom in the 8th century, called it "the land of gold."

Ghana's power peaked between roughly the 9th and 11th centuries, when its rulers taxed both the gold moving north and the salt moving south, amassing wealth that awed foreign visitors. The capital, Koumbi Saleh, grew into a twin city — one quarter for the king's court and traditional religion, another for Muslim merchants and their mosques — reflecting an empire that governed a diverse, commercially minded population while its kings retained indigenous religious authority. Royal power rested on a professional army, control of gold as a royal monopoly, and tribute from subordinate chiefdoms.

From the 11th century, Ghana faced mounting pressure: rival trade routes diverted commerce, prolonged drought strained agriculture and pasture, and incursions associated with the Almoravid movement disrupted the empire's political cohesion, though historians now see this decline as gradual rather than a sudden conquest. Weakened and fragmented, Ghana lost its dominant position to rising Soninke and Mande successor states, and by 1235 its remaining territory was absorbed into the newly ascendant Mali Empire following the Battle of Kirina. Though the state itself vanished, its model of a gold-and-salt trading empire set the template that Mali and later Songhai would follow.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Carolingian Empire rose and fractured, giving way to the feudal kingdoms of medieval Christendom.
  • Asia: China passed through the Tang and Song dynasties while the Islamic Abbasid Caliphate flourished in the Middle East.
  • North America: the Ancestral Puebloans built the great houses of Chaco Canyon, and Cahokia rose as a major Mississippian center.
