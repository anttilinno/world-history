Lapita culture

Oceania · 1600 BCE – 500 BCE

Around 1600 BCE, a distinctive culture emerged in the Bismarck Archipelago off New Guinea, marked by pottery decorated with intricate geometric patterns pressed into wet clay with toothed stamps. Named after a site in New Caledonia where archaeologists first identified it, the Lapita culture combined Southeast Asian ancestry with local New Guinea populations, producing seafaring communities equipped with outrigger canoes, domesticated pigs, dogs, and chickens, and a farming toolkit of taro, yam, and breadfruit.

What set the Lapita people apart was their extraordinary capacity for open-ocean voyaging. Within a few centuries they pushed east through the Solomon Islands and Vanuatu, and by around 1100–900 BCE had reached New Caledonia, Fiji, Tonga, and Samoa — crossing hundreds of kilometers of open sea with no land in sight, guided by knowledge of stars, swells, and bird flight. This expansion marked humanity's first settlement of Remote Oceania, a feat of navigation and colonization unmatched anywhere else in the world at the time.

Lapita communities left behind coastal villages, often built on stilts over lagoons, and their signature dentate-stamped pottery spread across a vast triangle of islands stretching some 4,000 kilometers. Trade networks carried obsidian, shell ornaments, and pottery styles between distant island groups, binding these scattered settlements into a shared cultural sphere even as local environments diverged sharply from one another.

By around 500 BCE, the elaborate stamped pottery style had faded, and Lapita communities in Fiji, Tonga, and Samoa had diversified into distinct local cultures — the direct ancestors of Polynesian society. The Lapita achievement, having supplied the language, seafaring technology, and social foundations of Polynesia, set the stage for later voyages that would carry their descendants across the remainder of the Pacific, ultimately reaching Hawaii, Easter Island, and New Zealand.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's New Kingdom pharaohs, including Ramesses II, ruled an empire stretching into the Levant.
  • Asia: China's Shang dynasty gave way to the Zhou, while Assyria built a formidable empire in Mesopotamia.
  • Europe: the Bronze Age collapse reshaped the Aegean, followed by the rise of Archaic Greek city-states.
