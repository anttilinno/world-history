Fertile Crescent agriculture

Asia · 10000 BCE – 4000 BCE

In the hill country and river valleys arcing from the Levant through upper Mesopotamia to the Zagros mountains, bands of hunter-gatherers began around 10000 BCE to settle near stands of wild wheat and barley they had long harvested. Sites such as Jericho and Göbekli Tepe show communities investing in permanent architecture — houses, granaries, even monumental ritual structures — before farming itself was fully established, suggesting settled life and agriculture developed together rather than one simply causing the other.

Over the following millennia, deliberate cultivation of einkorn and emmer wheat, barley, lentils, and peas replaced casual gathering, while sheep and goats were domesticated from local wild herds, followed by cattle and pigs. This bundle of crops and animals, later called the "Neolithic package," let populations grow far denser than foraging could support and spread outward from the Fertile Crescent into Anatolia, the Balkans, and eventually across Europe and into the Indus region.

By around 6000 BCE, villages across the region relied on farming and herding as their economic base, supported by new technologies including pottery, textiles, and irrigation in the drier lowlands. This shift from foraging to food production is conventionally called the Neolithic Revolution: it did not happen overnight, but by 4000 BCE it had laid the groundwork for the irrigation-fed surpluses, population density, and social complexity that would soon give rise to the first cities and states in nearby Sumer.

The Fertile Crescent's domesticated crops and livestock, along with the villages and techniques developed to manage them, became the template for agricultural societies across western Eurasia. Its legacy endures directly in the wheat fields, sheep flocks, and goat herds still central to agriculture from the Mediterranean to Central Asia today.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: hunter-gatherers along the Nile were beginning to exploit wild grains and fish, while communities further south experimented with cattle-keeping in the Sahara's then-green grasslands.
  • East Asia: rice was independently being domesticated along China's Yangtze River, and millet cultivation was taking hold in the Yellow River valley.
  • Americas: Paleoindian hunters were still tracking megafauna across the continent, and early experiments with squash cultivation were only just beginning in Mesoamerica.
