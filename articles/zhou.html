Zhou dynasty

Asia · 1046 BCE – 256 BCE

In 1046 BCE, King Wu of Zhou led a coalition of western states against the Shang dynasty, defeating them at the Battle of Muye and founding a new royal house that would rule, at least in name, for nearly eight centuries — the longest-lived dynasty in Chinese history. The early Zhou kings justified their conquest with the doctrine of the Mandate of Heaven, arguing that Heaven withdraws its favor from corrupt rulers and grants it to virtuous ones. This idea became a permanent fixture of Chinese political thought, used to legitimize (and delegitimize) every dynasty that followed.

The Western Zhou (1046–771 BCE) governed through a network of feudal lords bound to the king by kinship and ritual obligation, but this system gradually decentralized as regional states grew powerful in their own right. After the Zhou capital fell to invading forces in 771 BCE, the court retreated east to Luoyang, beginning the Eastern Zhou period, during which royal authority became largely ceremonial while real power passed to competing states.

The Eastern Zhou is remembered less for its politics than for its thought: the Spring and Autumn period (771–476 BCE) and Warring States period (475–221 BCE) produced the Hundred Schools of Thought, an extraordinary flowering of philosophy amid constant warfare. Confucius articulated an ethics of duty, ritual, and benevolent government; Laozi and the early Daoists proposed harmony with the natural order; Mozi, Han Feizi, Sun Tzu, and others offered rival visions of morality, statecraft, and war. These schools shaped Chinese civilization more durably than any Zhou king.

Zhou rule ended in 256 BCE when the state of Qin, one of the rising powers that had eclipsed the royal house, extinguished the last Zhou king. Qin then conquered its rival states and unified China in 221 BCE, but the intellectual legacy of the Zhou era — above all Confucianism — went on to shape Chinese government and society for the next two thousand years.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's New Kingdom was giving way to the Third Intermediate Period, and Kush was rising in Nubia.
  • Europe: Mycenaean Greece collapsed near the Zhou conquest's start, and by its end Rome had grown from a small republic into the dominant power of Italy.
  • Asia: the Assyrian and later Persian Achaemenid empires rose and fell in Mesopotamia, and the Maurya Empire unified much of India.
