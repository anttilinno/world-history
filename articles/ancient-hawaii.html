Ancient Hawaii

Oceania · 1000 – 1778

Polynesian voyagers, sailing double-hulled canoes navigated by stars, swells, and bird flight, reached the isolated Hawaiian archipelago roughly a thousand years ago, likely from the Marquesas and later Tahiti. Carrying pigs, dogs, chickens, and starter plants like taro, breadfruit, and sweet potato, these settlers built a self-sufficient society across islands separated from any other inhabited land by thousands of miles of open ocean.

Over succeeding centuries, Hawaiian society grew increasingly complex and stratified. Each island was divided into ahupuaa, wedge-shaped land divisions running from mountain to sea that supplied a community with everything from timber to fish, managed through sophisticated aquaculture (fishponds) and irrigated terrace farming. A rigid class system emerged, with alii (chiefs) claiming divine descent and ruling over makaainana (commoners) and kahuna (priestly experts), all bound by a strict code of kapu (taboo) governing behavior, gender separation, and sacred authority.

By the centuries before European contact, competing chiefdoms on Hawaii, Maui, Oahu, and Kauai waged frequent warfare, building massive heiau (temple platforms) and consolidating power under paramount chiefs such as Umi-a-Liloa and later Kamehameha's predecessors. This was a civilization of oral tradition, hula, elaborate genealogical chant, and monumental engineering, entirely without metal tools or writing.

Isolation ended abruptly in 1778, when British explorer James Cook became the first European to reach the islands, naming them the Sandwich Islands. Contact brought devastating introduced diseases and set in motion rapid political and cultural change, as Kamehameha I would soon unify the islands under a single kingdom, closing the era of independent, isolated Hawaiian chiefdoms.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Vikings were raiding and settling, and by 1778 the Enlightenment and American Revolution were in full swing.
  • Asia: China cycled through the Song, Yuan, Ming, and Qing dynasties over this span.
  • North America: the Mississippian culture built Cahokia, while later the founding of the United States was underway in 1776.
