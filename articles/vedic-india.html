Vedic India

Asia · 1500 BCE – 500 BCE

As the cities of the Indus Valley civilization faded, Indo-Aryan-speaking peoples moved into the plains of northwest India, bringing with them a pastoral, cattle-herding culture and an oral religious tradition. Over the following centuries their priests composed the Vedas — the Rigveda, Samaveda, Yajurveda, and Atharvaveda — hymns, rituals, and chants preserved with extraordinary precision through memorization and recitation. These texts, later regarded as revealed scripture (shruti), form the oldest layer of Hindu religious literature and give the era its name.

The early Vedic period centered on the Punjab and the Sarasvati-Ghaggar river system, with society organized around clans, cattle wealth, and fire sacrifices (yajna) offered to gods such as Indra, Agni, and Varuna. By the later Vedic period (c. 1000–500 BCE), settlement shifted east into the fertile Ganges-Yamuna doab, iron tools spread, agriculture and settled kingdoms replaced pastoral mobility, and society hardened into the four-fold varna system of priests, warriors, commoners, and servants. Political life coalesced into janapadas — territorial chiefdoms and early states ruled by kings advised by Brahmin priests.

The period's end brought profound intellectual ferment: the philosophical Upanishads reworked Vedic ritualism into speculation on the self (atman), ultimate reality (brahman), and karma and rebirth, laying the conceptual foundation for classical Hinduism. This questioning climate also produced rival movements — Buddhism and Jainism arose around the 6th century BCE partly in reaction to Vedic sacrificial religion and caste hierarchy. Sanskrit, refined and standardized in this era, became the enduring liturgical and literary language of Indian civilization, and the social and religious structures forged under Vedic rule shaped South Asian life for millennia to come.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's New Kingdom pharaohs gave way to Nubian and Assyrian rule as the Bronze Age order collapsed.
  • Europe: Mycenaean Greece fell, ushering in a dark age later followed by the rise of Archaic Greek city-states.
  • Asia: China's Shang dynasty gave way to the Zhou, while Neo-Assyrian and then Persian empires dominated the Near East.
