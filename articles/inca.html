Inca Empire

South America · 1438 – 1533

The Inca began as a small highland kingdom centered on Cusco, in what is now Peru. In 1438, the ruler Pachacuti defeated a rival Chanka invasion and launched a campaign of conquest and diplomacy that transformed Cusco's chiefdom into Tawantinsuyu, "the four regions" — an empire that would eventually stretch some 4,000 km along the Andes, from southern Colombia to central Chile.

At its height, the Inca Empire governed perhaps 10 million people across deserts, high plateaus, and rainforest margins without a written language, the wheel, or draft animals larger than the llama. Its achievements were logistical and organizational: over 40,000 km of stone-paved roads linked the empire, relayed by chasqui runners; terraced agriculture turned mountainsides into farmland; and administrators tracked labor, tribute, and census data using the khipu, a system of knotted and colored cords. Labor was organized through the mit'a system, a rotational tax paid in work rather than goods, which built roads, temples, and storehouses such as those at the capital and at Machu Picchu, a royal estate constructed under Pachacuti.

The empire's rapid expansion also left it fragile. A war of succession between the half-brothers Huáscar and Atahualpa, compounded by smallpox spreading ahead of European contact, weakened the state just as Spanish conquistadors arrived. Francisco Pizarro captured Atahualpa at Cajamarca in 1532 and had him executed in 1533, and Spanish forces took Cusco later that year, effectively ending independent Inca rule, though resistance continued from the remote Vilcabamba region until 1572.

Despite its brief span as a unified empire, Inca engineering and administration left a lasting imprint on the Andes: many colonial and modern roads, terraces, and irrigation systems still follow Inca foundations, and Quechua, the empire's administrative language, remains widely spoken today.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Renaissance was in full flower, Gutenberg had just printed his Bible, and Constantinople had fallen to the Ottomans.
  • Asia: Ming China was retreating from overseas exploration after Zheng He's voyages, and the Ottoman Empire was expanding under Suleiman the Magnificent.
  • North America: the Aztec Empire was near its peak in the Valley of Mexico, falling to Hernán Cortés just over a decade before the Inca did.
