Babylon

Asia · 1894 BCE – 539 BCE

Babylon rose on the Euphrates around 1894 BCE, when an Amorite chieftain named Sumu-abum founded a modest city-state amid the older, established powers of southern Mesopotamia. For over a century it remained a minor player, overshadowed by rivals such as Larsa, Isin, and Mari, until the sixth king of its First Dynasty transformed its fortunes entirely.

That king, Hammurabi (r. c. 1792–1750 BCE), conquered his neighbors one by one and unified Mesopotamia under Babylonian rule. His enduring legacy is the Code of Hammurabi, a monumental stele inscribed with nearly 300 laws governing trade, property, family, and punishment, organized on the principle of proportional retribution — "an eye for an eye." Babylon became the region's preeminent city, a center of law, astronomy, mathematics, and literature, and the Akkadian dialect spoken there became the diplomatic lingua franca of the ancient Near East for centuries afterward.

The dynasty declined after Hammurabi's death, and a Hittite raid sacked the city around 1595 BCE, ushering in centuries of Kassite and Assyrian domination. Babylon's second golden age arrived with the Neo-Babylonian Empire, founded by Nabopolassar in 626 BCE after he threw off Assyrian rule. His son Nebuchadnezzar II (r. 605–562 BCE) rebuilt the city on a spectacular scale, raising the glazed-brick Ishtar Gate and, according to later tradition, the Hanging Gardens for his homesick queen. Nebuchadnezzar also conquered Judah and deported its elite to Babylon, the biblical Babylonian Captivity.

The empire did not long outlive him. In 539 BCE, Cyrus the Great of Persia took Babylon with little resistance, absorbing it into the Achaemenid Empire. The city endured for centuries more as a provincial capital, but its age of independent power had ended, leaving behind law, astronomy, and myth that shaped Western civilization long after its walls crumbled.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's Middle Kingdom gave way to the New Kingdom, whose pharaohs built Karnak and campaigned into the Levant.
  • Europe: Minoan Crete flourished and then collapsed, followed by the rise and fall of Mycenaean Greece and a subsequent Dark Age.
  • Americas: the Olmec civilization emerged along Mexico's Gulf Coast, raising colossal stone heads and laying groundwork for later Mesoamerican cultures.
