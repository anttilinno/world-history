Songhai Empire

Africa · 1464 – 1591

The Songhai people had long controlled the middle Niger River around the city of Gao, first as a tributary of the Mali Empire and then as an increasingly independent power. When Mali's authority weakened in the fifteenth century, the Songhai ruler Sonni Ali (r. 1464–1492) broke free and began an aggressive campaign of conquest, seizing the great trading cities of Timbuktu and Jenne and building the largest state West Africa had yet seen.

Sonni Ali's successor, Askia Muhammad, who seized power in 1493, consolidated and expanded the empire further, extending Songhai control over the trans-Saharan trade routes that carried gold, salt, and slaves between West Africa and the Mediterranean world. Under the Askia dynasty, Timbuktu and Jenne flourished as centers of Islamic scholarship, law, and commerce, home to renowned universities and libraries that drew students and scholars from across the Muslim world. The empire's wealth rested on control of gold-producing regions to the south and the taxation of merchants moving goods across the Sahara.

Songhai's administration organized its vast territory into provinces governed by appointed officials, supported by a standing army and a navy of boats that patrolled the Niger. Succession disputes and internal rebellions weakened central authority in the later sixteenth century, leaving the empire vulnerable just as an external threat emerged from the north.

In 1591, a Moroccan army equipped with firearms crossed the Sahara and defeated Songhai's larger but more traditionally armed forces at the Battle of Tondibi. The invasion shattered Songhai's political unity, and the empire fragmented into smaller successor states, ending the last of the great medieval West African empires and shifting regional trade patterns as European maritime routes increasingly bypassed the Saharan caravan trade.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Reformation was splitting Christendom, and Spain and Portugal were carving up newly reached American territories.
  • Asia: the Ottoman Empire was near its height under Suleiman the Magnificent, and Ming China ruled from Beijing.
  • North America: the Aztec Empire rose and fell to Spanish conquest within Songhai's own lifespan.
