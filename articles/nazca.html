Nazca

South America · 100 CE – 800 CE

The Nazca culture emerged around 100 CE in the arid river valleys of southern Peru's coastal desert, inheriting artistic and technological traditions from the earlier Paracas culture. Settling along the Rio Grande de Nazca drainage, the Nazca built no great cities of stone but instead developed a highly organized society centered on irrigation, ceremonial gathering places, and a distinctive artistic tradition expressed above all in polychrome pottery and textiles.

The Nazca are best known for the Nazca Lines, hundreds of enormous geoglyphs etched into the desert floor by removing dark, oxidized surface stones to reveal the lighter sediment beneath. These include vast straight lines and trapezoids running for kilometers as well as figurative designs — a hummingbird, a monkey, a spider, a killer whale — some over 100 meters across, best appreciated from the air. Their purpose remains debated, with theories ranging from astronomical alignments to ritual pathways tied to water and fertility. The Nazca also engineered puquios, underground aqueducts that tapped subterranean water to sustain agriculture in one of the driest places on Earth, and produced richly painted ceramics depicting deities, animals, and trophy heads.

From around 500 CE, prolonged drought and possibly environmental degradation from deforestation strained Nazca society, weakening its ceremonial centers such as Cahuachi. By 800 CE the culture had faded, absorbed into the broader currents of Andean civilization that eventually fed into the Wari and, much later, Inca states. The Nazca Lines endured undisturbed in the rainless desert for over a millennium, rediscovered by modern aviators and now recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of archaeology's most striking puzzles.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Roman Empire reached its height and later began to fracture under pressure from Germanic peoples.
  • Asia: China cycled through the Han, Three Kingdoms, and Tang dynasties amid the spread of Buddhism.
  • Africa: the kingdom of Aksum in the Horn of Africa rose as a major trading power minting its own coinage.
