Kingdom of Aksum

Africa · 100 CE – 940 CE

Rising in the highlands of what is now northern Ethiopia and Eritrea, Aksum grew out of earlier Dʿmt and South Arabian-influenced cultures to become the dominant power of the Red Sea region by the first century CE. Its capital, Aksum, and its outlet port at Adulis anchored a trade network linking the Roman Mediterranean, the Nile valley, Arabia, Persia, and India, dealing in ivory, gold, incense, and slaves. The third-century Persian prophet Mani reportedly ranked Aksum among the four great powers of the world, alongside Rome, Persia, and China.

Aksum developed its own Ge'ez script, minted its own gold, silver, and bronze coinage — a rarity among ancient African states — and raised towering carved stone stelae to mark royal graves, the largest reaching over 30 meters. Around 330 CE, King Ezana converted to Christianity, making Aksum one of the earliest states in the world to adopt the faith as an official religion, alongside Armenia. Aksumite armies extended influence across the Red Sea into southern Arabia, including a sixth-century campaign under King Kaleb that toppled a persecuting Himyarite king in Yemen.

From the seventh century, the rise of Islam and the redirection of trade routes eroded Aksum's commercial dominance, cutting it off from its Mediterranean and Arabian markets. Combined with environmental strain and possible internal upheaval, the kingdom contracted and its political center shifted south into the Ethiopian highlands. By around 940 CE, the Aksumite state had effectively dissolved, giving way to successor dynasties, but it left behind an enduring Christian, literate, and monumental legacy that later Ethiopian kingdoms, including the Zagwe and Solomonic dynasties, claimed as their own.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Roman Empire was near its height, later fracturing into western collapse and Byzantine continuity.
  • Asia: China cycled through the Han, Three Kingdoms, and Tang dynasties as the Silk Road flourished.
  • North America: Maya city-states such as Tikal and Copán rose, peaked, and began their classic-era decline.
