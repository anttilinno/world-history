United States

North America · 1776 – present

In 1776, thirteen British colonies along the Atlantic seaboard declared independence, citing Enlightenment ideals of natural rights and self-government. Victory in the Revolutionary War, secured with French assistance and confirmed by the Treaty of Paris in 1783, produced a fragile confederation that was reorganized in 1787–1789 under a new Constitution establishing a federal republic with checks between executive, legislative, and judicial branches.

The young republic expanded relentlessly westward through purchase, treaty, and conquest — the Louisiana Purchase of 1803, the displacement of Native nations, and war with Mexico in 1846–1848 pushed its borders to the Pacific. This growth was built on a deepening contradiction between liberty and slavery, which tore the country apart in the Civil War (1861–1865); the Union's victory ended slavery but left Reconstruction's promise of racial equality unfulfilled for a century more.

Industrialization turned the United States into the world's largest economy by 1900, and two world wars left it the dominant Western power, anchoring the postwar order alongside the Soviet Union in a Cold War rivalry that shaped global politics until 1991. Domestically, the twentieth century brought the New Deal welfare state, the Civil Rights Movement's dismantling of legal segregation, and continued waves of immigration that remade American society.

Since the Cold War's end, the United States has remained the world's preeminent military and economic power, driving global technology and culture from Silicon Valley to Hollywood, while grappling with rising inequality, political polarization, and debates over its role abroad — from Middle East wars to strategic competition with China.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the French Revolution erupted in 1789, followed by the Napoleonic Wars that redrew the continent's map.
  • Asia: the Qing dynasty ruled China for over a century after 1776 before its collapse in 1912 ushered in a turbulent modern era.
  • Africa: European powers carved up the continent in the late 1800s Scramble for Africa, a process largely undone by decolonization after 1945.
