Ming dynasty

Asia · 1368 – 1644

In 1368, the rebel leader Zhu Yuanzhang, a former peasant and Buddhist monk, drove out the Mongol Yuan dynasty and founded the Ming dynasty, taking the reign name Hongwu. He rebuilt a war-ravaged China around a strongly centralized state, reviving Confucian civil-service examinations, restoring agriculture, and ruling with harsh discipline against corruption and rival power centers, including his own ministers.

His son the Yongle Emperor moved the capital to Beijing and built the Forbidden City, completed in 1420, as the seat of imperial power for the rest of the dynasty. Yongle also sponsored the mariner Zheng He, whose immense treasure fleets sailed as far as Southeast Asia, India, the Arabian Peninsula, and East Africa between 1405 and 1433, projecting Chinese power and gathering tribute and trade far beyond any earlier Chinese naval effort. The Ming era also produced the Great Wall's stone-faced modern form, a flourishing porcelain and silk trade, and celebrated novels such as Journey to the West and Water Margin.

After Yongle's death, the court increasingly turned inward: the treasure fleets were discontinued, private maritime trade was restricted, and resources shifted toward defending the northern frontier against Mongol raids. Later Ming rule suffered from weak emperors, factional strife between officials and eunuchs, and fiscal strain, compounded by climate-driven famines in the 17th century.

Peasant rebellions spread through the 1630s and early 1640s, and in 1644 the rebel Li Zicheng captured Beijing, prompting the last Ming emperor to hang himself. Manchu forces of the Qing dynasty soon seized the opportunity to take the capital, ending Ming rule on the mainland, though Ming loyalist regimes persisted in the south for decades afterward.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Renaissance flowered in Italy, then gave way to the Reformation, the age of exploration, and the Thirty Years' War.
  • Africa: the Songhai Empire rose along the Niger River and fell to Moroccan invasion in 1591.
  • North America: the Aztec and Inca empires flourished and were conquered by Spanish forces in the early 1500s.
