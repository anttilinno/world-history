Crusades

Europe · 1096 CE – 1291 CE

In 1095, Pope Urban II called on the knights of Latin Christendom to march east and reclaim Jerusalem from Muslim rule, framing the campaign as an armed pilgrimage that offered spiritual reward for military service. The First Crusade, launched in 1096, succeeded beyond expectation: crusader armies captured Antioch and then Jerusalem in 1099, carving out a string of Christian states along the eastern Mediterranean coast — the so-called Crusader States, including the Kingdom of Jerusalem.

Holding these gains proved far harder than winning them. The fall of Edessa to Muslim forces in 1144 triggered the failed Second Crusade, and Saladin's decisive victory at Hattin in 1187, followed by his recapture of Jerusalem, provoked the Third Crusade, in which Richard the Lionheart, Philip II of France, and Frederick Barbarossa fought Saladin to a stalemate. Later expeditions drifted further from their original aim: the Fourth Crusade of 1204 notoriously sacked Constantinople, a Christian city, gutting the Byzantine Empire and deepening the schism between Eastern and Western Christianity rather than confronting Islam at all.

Beyond these major campaigns, the crusading idea spread to sanction wars against pagans in the Baltic, Muslims in Iberia (the Reconquista), and heretics in southern France, while children's crusades and popular movements revealed how deeply the ideology had permeated European society. In the Levant, however, Christian territory steadily contracted as Mamluk Egypt reorganized the region's defenses; the fall of Acre in 1291 extinguished the last crusader foothold on the mainland.

The Crusades reshaped the medieval world on both sides: they intensified trade and cultural exchange between Europe and the Islamic Middle East, enriched Italian maritime republics like Venice and Genoa, hardened Christian-Muslim and Christian-Jewish relations, and left a legacy of holy war that has echoed through European and Middle Eastern memory ever since.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Song dynasty flourished in China even as the Mongol Empire rose under Genghis Khan and his successors, eventually reshaping much of Eurasia.
  • Africa: the Mali Empire was consolidating power in West Africa, and Great Zimbabwe was rising as a trading center in the south.
  • North America: the Ancestral Puebloans built the great houses of Chaco Canyon, and Cahokia grew into the largest city north of Mexico.
