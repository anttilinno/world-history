Industrial Revolution

Europe · 1760 – 1914

Beginning around 1760 in Britain, the Industrial Revolution transformed economies built on human and animal muscle, wood, and water into ones powered by coal and iron machinery. Cheap coal, accessible iron ore, a growing merchant class, and an agricultural revolution that freed up labor combined to make Britain the first industrial nation. Innovations in textiles — the spinning jenny, the water frame, and the power loom — moved cloth production from cottages into factories, while James Watt's improved steam engine, patented in 1769, gave industry a source of power independent of rivers and weather.

Steam power spread from textile mills to mining, ironworking, and eventually transport. The Stockton and Darlington Railway opened in 1825 and the Liverpool and Manchester Railway in 1830, launching a rail-building boom that shrank distances and knit national markets together; steamships did the same for oceans. Iron and later steel production surged, cities like Manchester and Birmingham swelled with factory workers, and a new industrial working class emerged alongside a wealthy manufacturing bourgeoisie. By the mid-1800s the revolution had spread to Belgium, France, Germany, and the United States, each industrializing on its own timeline.

A second wave from roughly 1870 onward, often called the Second Industrial Revolution, added steel, electricity, chemicals, and the internal combustion engine, along with new organizational forms like the factory assembly line and large corporations. This era also brought profound social costs — child labor, dangerous working conditions, and crowded slums — that spurred labor movements, trade unions, and early welfare legislation across Europe.

By 1914, on the eve of the First World War, industrial capacity had become a measure of national power, and factories, railways, and mass production had reshaped daily life across Europe and beyond. The revolution's legacy — urbanization, consumer goods, mechanized warfare, and sustained economic growth — set the template for the modern industrial and post-industrial world.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Qing dynasty in China and Tokugawa Japan largely resisted industrialization until forced open by Western powers, with Japan then rapidly industrializing after the 1868 Meiji Restoration.
  • North America: the United States industrialized through the 19th century, fought its Civil War (1861–1865), and expanded westward via railroads built with the same steam technology.
  • Africa: European powers used industrial-era weapons, steamships, and railways to carry out the Scramble for Africa, colonizing most of the continent by 1914.
