Cold War & European Union

Europe · 1945 – present

World War II left Europe devastated and divided. From 1945, the continent split along an "Iron Curtain": a Soviet-dominated bloc in the east, ruled through communist parties and cemented by the Warsaw Pact in 1955, faced a Western bloc anchored by NATO (1949) and American economic aid under the Marshall Plan. Germany itself was partitioned, its former capital Berlin walled in two from 1961, becoming the Cold War's starkest symbol.

In the west, former enemies pursued integration to make future war unthinkable. The 1951 Coal and Steel Community bound France and West Germany's war industries together; the 1957 Treaty of Rome created the European Economic Community. Successive enlargements and treaties — the Single European Act, Maastricht in 1992 founding the European Union, the euro currency in 1999–2002, and the Schengen open-border zone — built a deepening political and economic union spanning most of the continent.

The Cold War ended abruptly: reform in the USSR under Gorbachev, popular uprisings across Eastern Europe, and the fall of the Berlin Wall in November 1989 led to German reunification in 1990 and the Soviet Union's dissolution in 1991. Former communist states joined NATO and the EU through the 2000s and 2010s, seemingly closing the continent's great division, even as Britain voted to leave the EU in 2016 (completed 2020).

That post-Cold War settlement fractured when Russia annexed Crimea in 2014 and launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, the largest European war since 1945. The EU responded with sanctions, military aid, and Ukraine's candidacy for membership, while Finland and Sweden abandoned neutrality to join NATO — reopening questions about Europe's borders and security that many had thought settled forever.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: China's Communist Party won its civil war in 1949, while Japan rebuilt into an economic power under postwar pacifism.
  • Africa: decolonization swept the continent from the 1950s–1970s, producing dozens of new independent states.
  • North America: the United States and Soviet Union waged the wider Cold War through proxy conflicts, an arms race, and the Space Race.
