Shang dynasty

Asia · 1600 BCE – 1046 BCE

The Shang dynasty emerged in the Yellow River valley of North China around 1600 BCE, succeeding the semi-legendary Xia as the earliest Chinese dynasty confirmed by archaeological and written evidence. Shang kings ruled from a series of capitals, the last and best-known being Yin, near modern Anyang, excavated since the 1920s and rich in royal tombs, palace foundations, and workshops.

Shang society was organized around a warrior aristocracy and an elaborate cult of ancestor worship, in which kings consulted the spirits through pyromancy: heating turtle plastrons and ox scapulae until they cracked, then inscribing the questions and answers on the bone. Tens of thousands of these oracle bones survive, preserving the oldest known body of Chinese writing, a direct ancestor of the modern script. Shang artisans also achieved extraordinary mastery of bronze casting, producing ritual vessels of great technical and artistic sophistication used in sacrifices to royal ancestors, alongside advances in chariot warfare, silk production, and a sophisticated calendar tracking lunar months and solar years.

The dynasty's authority rested on control of bronze production and military force projected outward from its core territory, with vassal lords and frequent campaigns against neighboring peoples. By the 11th century BCE, the last Shang king, Di Xin, had become a byword for cruelty and excess in later tradition, and his rule was overthrown around 1046 BCE at the Battle of Muye by the Zhou, a formerly subject people from the west.

Though the Zhou conquest ended Shang political power, its legacy proved foundational: the Zhou justified their rule with the concept of the Mandate of Heaven, and Chinese writing, bronze ritual culture, and ancestor veneration all descended directly from Shang practice, shaping Chinese civilization for millennia afterward.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's New Kingdom pharaohs, including Hatshepsut and Ramesses II, were building an empire along the Nile.
  • Europe: Mycenaean Greece was flourishing, and the Minoan civilization on Crete was in decline after volcanic eruption at Thera.
  • Americas: the Olmec civilization was taking shape along Mexico's Gulf Coast, and Andean societies were building early ceremonial centers.
