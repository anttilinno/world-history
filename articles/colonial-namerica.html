European colonies

North America · 1521 – 1776

Spanish conquest reached the North American mainland in 1521 with the fall of Tenochtitlan, and over the following century Spain pushed north from Mexico into Florida, the Southwest, and California, founding St. Augustine in 1565 and Santa Fe in 1610. French traders and missionaries established a rival network along the St. Lawrence and Mississippi, founding Quebec in 1608 and later New Orleans, built on alliances with Indigenous nations for the fur trade. The Dutch chartered a West India Company post at New Amsterdam in 1624, and the English planted Jamestown in 1607 and Plymouth in 1620, beginning a wave of colonies along the Atlantic seaboard.

By the mid-1600s these rival empires were competing directly: England seized New Amsterdam from the Dutch in 1664, renaming it New York, while French and English settlers and their Indigenous allies clashed repeatedly from King Philip's War through the French and Indian War of 1754–1763, which ended French colonial power in Canada. Across the continent, Indigenous nations — the Haudenosaunee, Wabanaki, Powhatan, Pueblo peoples, and many others — were displaced, decimated by epidemic disease, or drawn into shifting alliances and trade dependencies as colonial populations grew through immigration and, in the plantation colonies, the forced labor of enslaved Africans.

English colonization proved the most demographically dominant, with thirteen colonies stretching from New England to Georgia developing distinct economies, legislatures, and identities under loose royal oversight. Resentment over taxation and governance after the French and Indian War fed a growing independence movement, culminating in the Declaration of Independence in 1776, which severed the thirteen colonies from Britain even as Spanish, French, and other European claims persisted elsewhere on the continent.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Reformation, the Thirty Years' War, and the rise of absolutist monarchies reshaped the continent.
  • Asia: the Ming dynasty fell to the Qing in 1644, and the Mughal Empire reached its territorial peak under Aurangzeb.
  • Africa: the Atlantic slave trade intensified, feeding coastal states like Dahomey and the Asante Empire.
