First South Americans

South America · 15000 BCE – 10000 BCE

How and when humans first reached South America remains one of the great open questions in archaeology, but by 15,000 BCE small bands of hunter-gatherers had already pushed deep into the continent — far sooner and farther than the older "Clovis first" model of American migration allowed. Their ancestors had crossed from Siberia into Beringia and down through the Americas, likely following a Pacific coastal route rather than waiting for an inland ice-free corridor to open, since sites in South America predate that corridor's availability.

The clearest evidence comes from Monte Verde in southern Chile, a waterlogged bog site that preserved wood, hide, and plant remains normally lost to time. Radiocarbon dates place people there by roughly 14,500 BCE, with hints of an even older occupation layer beneath it. Monte Verde's inhabitants built wooden structures covered in animal hides, used a wide range of medicinal and edible plants gathered from far-flung environments, and worked stone and mastodon ivory — evidence of a settled, resourceful society rather than a fleeting hunting party.

Other early sites, from Pedra Furada in Brazil to Huaca Prieta on the Peruvian coast and Los Toldos in Patagonia, suggest that people spread rapidly across South America's varied terrain — coastlines, highlands, and grasslands — within a few thousand years of arrival. They hunted now-extinct megafauna such as giant ground sloths and native horses alongside smaller game, and fished and foraged along rivers and shores.

By 10,000 BCE, as the Ice Age gave way to warmer Holocene conditions and megafauna died out across the Americas, descendants of these earliest South Americans had laid the groundwork for the regional cultures that followed, eventually giving rise to the settled farming and fishing communities of the Andes and coastal Peru.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: Ice Age hunters were painting the cave galleries of Lascaux and Altamira as glaciers slowly retreated.
  • Asia: in Japan, some of the world's earliest pottery was emerging among Jomon-period foragers.
  • North America: Clovis and pre-Clovis hunters were spreading across the continent, pursuing mammoths and other megafauna.
