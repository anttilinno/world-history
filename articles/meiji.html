Meiji Japan

Asia · 1868 – 1912

In 1868, a coalition of samurai from Satsuma and Chōshū overthrew the Tokugawa shogunate and restored the emperor Meiji to nominal supreme authority, ending over two and a half centuries of feudal isolation. The new government's rallying cry, "rich country, strong army," reflected its founding anxiety: Western gunboats had forced Japan open in the 1850s, and China's humiliation in the Opium Wars showed what awaited a nation that failed to modernize on its own terms.

The Meiji leadership abolished the feudal domains and the samurai class itself, built a conscript army and navy on Prussian and British models, and sent missions abroad to study Western law, education, and industry. Within a generation Japan had a constitution (1889), a national rail and telegraph network, state-sponsored textile mills and shipyards, and universal schooling. This compressed industrialization was funded partly by land taxes and partly by selling state enterprises to politically connected industrial combines, the zaibatsu, which would dominate the Japanese economy for decades.

Military success confirmed the transformation: Japan defeated Qing China in 1894–95, annexing Taiwan, and stunned the world by defeating Russia in 1904–05, the first modern victory of an Asian power over a European one. Korea was annexed outright in 1910. By the time the Meiji emperor died in 1912, Japan had renegotiated the unequal treaties imposed by the West and stood as a recognized great power, though the same militarized, oligarchic institutions that drove this rise would later steer Japan toward empire and war.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Scramble for Africa was being formalized at the 1884–85 Berlin Conference.
  • North America: the United States was closing its frontier and entering the Gilded Age of industrial expansion.
  • Africa: the Mahdist state was resisting British and Egyptian control in Sudan.
