World Wars

Europe · 1914 – 1945

The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo in June 1914 triggered a web of alliances that dragged Europe's great powers into war within weeks. What followed was four years of industrialized slaughter: machine guns, artillery, poison gas, and entrenched trench lines from the North Sea to the Alps turned the Western Front into a killing ground where battles like the Somme and Verdun each cost hundreds of thousands of lives for negligible territorial gain. The United States entered in 1917, Russia collapsed into revolution and withdrew, and by November 1918 exhausted Germany sought an armistice. The war killed roughly 17 million people and toppled the German, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, and Ottoman empires.

The 1919 Treaty of Versailles imposed harsh reparations and territorial losses on Germany, fueling resentment that, combined with the Great Depression, propelled Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party to power in 1933. Fascist Italy and militarist Japan pursued their own campaigns of conquest through the 1930s, and Germany's invasion of Poland in September 1939 began a second global conflict far more destructive than the first.

World War II engulfed nearly every region on earth. Nazi Germany overran continental Europe before stalling in the Soviet Union, whose Red Army eventually turned the tide at Stalingrad and drove west to Berlin. In the Pacific, Japan's expansion, launched with the attack on Pearl Harbor in December 1941, was rolled back by American island-hopping campaigns. The war was distinguished by the deliberate targeting of civilians, culminating in the Holocaust, in which Nazi Germany systematically murdered six million Jews and millions of others, and in the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945, which forced Japan's surrender.

Together the two wars killed an estimated 70 to 85 million people and left much of Europe and East Asia in ruins. The conflicts shattered European colonial dominance, accelerated decolonization across Africa and Asia, and gave rise to a new order centered on the United States and Soviet Union, formalized through institutions like the United Nations and soon hardening into the Cold War.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: Japan's imperial ambitions and China's civil war between Nationalists and Communists were reshaping East Asia even as the wars raged.
  • Africa: colonial powers conscripted African soldiers and resources for both wars, seeding the independence movements that would sweep the continent after 1945.
  • North America: the United States emerged from relative isolationism to become an industrial and military superpower, ending the war as the world's dominant economy.
