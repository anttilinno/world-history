Out-of-Africa migration

Africa · 70000 BCE – 45000 BCE

Anatomically modern humans, Homo sapiens, had existed in Africa for over 100,000 years before small groups began pushing beyond the continent in lasting numbers. Genetic and archaeological evidence points to a major dispersal beginning around 70,000 BCE, likely crossing the Bab-el-Mandeb strait at the mouth of the Red Sea into Arabia, though earlier, less successful forays out of Africa had happened before this window.

These populations were small — genetic bottlenecks suggest only a few thousand individuals founded the entire non-African lineage — but they carried a flexible toolkit of language, symbolic behavior, and adaptable technology that let them exploit coastlines, grasslands, and forests unlike anything their hominin predecessors had managed. Following coastal routes and river corridors, they spread rapidly along the Indian Ocean rim, reaching South Asia and Southeast Asia within a few thousand years, and pushing into Australia by around 50,000 BCE, which required deliberate sea crossings.

Other branches moved into the Near East, Central Asia, and eventually Europe, where by roughly 45,000 BCE they were encountering and occasionally interbreeding with Neanderthal populations already established there. This expansion is the ancestral root of every non-African population alive today; nearly all people outside Africa trace the bulk of their ancestry to this event, with the resulting founder effect explaining why non-African populations carry less overall genetic diversity than Africans.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: Neanderthals still held the continent, adapted to Ice Age cold with their own tools and burial practices.
  • Asia: Denisovans occupied parts of Siberia and further east, later contributing DNA to some of the arriving migrants.
  • Americas and most of Oceania: entirely unpeopled, remaining so for tens of thousands more years until later migrations arrived.
