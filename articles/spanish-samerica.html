Spanish colonial rule

South America · 1533 – 1820

Spanish conquest of South America began in earnest in 1533, when Francisco Pizarro's small force captured Cuzco and toppled the Inca Empire, already weakened by a civil war and by smallpox that had raced ahead of the invaders along trade routes. Within decades, Spain had imposed the Viceroyalty of Peru over most of the continent's western and central regions, governing through a rigid hierarchy of viceroys, audiencias, and encomenderos who extracted labor and tribute from Indigenous communities.

The colony's wealth rested on silver. The mountain of Potosí, discovered in 1545 in present-day Bolivia, became the richest mine on earth, and the mercury-amalgamation process refined at nearby Huancavelica let Spain mint vast quantities of silver coin that financed its European wars and flowed onward through Manila to China. The forced labor draft known as the mita conscripted thousands of Indigenous men each year to work the mines and refineries under brutal conditions, while across the wider colony, disease — smallpox, measles, typhus, influenza — killed the great majority of the pre-contact Indigenous population within a century, the deadliest demographic collapse in recorded history.

Colonial society was reorganized around Catholic missions, forced resettlement into towns (reducciones), and a casta system that ranked people by descent, with Spanish-born peninsulares atop American-born criollos, mestizos, Indigenous peoples, and enslaved Africans brought in to replace lost native labor. Bourbon reforms in the 18th century tightened royal control and taxation, provoking revolts such as Túpac Amaru II's 1780 uprising, and by the early 1800s a criollo elite steeped in Enlightenment ideas and angered by Spain's crisis under Napoleon began the wars of independence that, through the 1810s and into the 1820s, dismantled three centuries of Spanish rule.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Reformation, the Thirty Years' War, and later the Enlightenment and Napoleonic Wars reshaped the continent that ruled this empire from afar.
  • Asia: the Ming dynasty gave way to the Qing in China, while the Mughal Empire rose and fractured in India and Tokugawa Japan sealed itself off from the world.
  • Africa: the Atlantic slave trade intensified along the west coast, feeding labor to the very silver and plantation economies Spain built in the Americas.
