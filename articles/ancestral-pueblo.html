Ancestral Puebloans

North America · 750 – 1350

By around 750 CE, farming communities across the arid Four Corners region of the American Southwest — where Colorado, Utah, Arizona, and New Mexico meet — had begun settling into permanent villages of pit houses, growing maize, beans, and squash, and firing distinctive black-on-white pottery. Earlier known to archaeologists as the Anasazi, these peoples are now called the Ancestral Puebloans, ancestors of today's Hopi, Zuni, and Rio Grande Pueblo nations.

The civilization reached its height between roughly 850 and 1150 CE at Chaco Canyon, New Mexico, where massive multi-story "great houses" such as Pueblo Bonito, containing hundreds of rooms, anchored a network of outlying communities linked by hundreds of miles of engineered roads. Chaco functioned as a ceremonial, political, and economic center, aligned with solar and lunar cycles and connected by trade to distant sources of turquoise, macaw feathers, and shell. Further north and west, communities at Mesa Verde built elaborate cliff dwellings tucked into sandstone alcoves, most famously Cliff Palace, constructed in the late 1100s and 1200s.

A prolonged drought beginning around 1130 CE, compounded by soil depletion, resource competition, and social upheaval, undermined Chaco's dominance, and by the late 1200s a severe multi-decade drought known as the "Great Drought" pushed communities to abandon Mesa Verde and the wider Four Corners area entirely. Populations migrated south and east to the Rio Grande valley, the Hopi mesas, and Zuni lands, where their descendants built new pueblos and carried Ancestral Puebloan traditions, architecture, and ceremonial practices forward into the present day.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Carolingian Empire rose and fragmented, giving way to the High Middle Ages and Gothic cathedral building.
  • Asia: China's Tang and Song dynasties flourished with printing and gunpowder, while the Mongol Empire later swept across the continent.
  • Africa: the Kingdom of Great Zimbabwe and the trans-Saharan empires of Ghana and Mali were amassing wealth through gold and trade.
