European exploration

Oceania · 1606 – 1788

In 1606, the Dutch captain Willem Janszoon sailed the VOC vessel Duyfken along the western coast of Cape York, becoming the first European known to sight and chart part of Australia. Over the following decades, ships of the Dutch East India Company, sailing the "Brouwer Route" across the Indian Ocean toward the East Indies, repeatedly struck the continent's western and southern shores, sometimes by design and sometimes by shipwreck. Dutch navigators charted long stretches of coastline and named the land New Holland, though they found little to justify colonization.

Abel Tasman's voyages of 1642–44 marked the high point of Dutch exploration, revealing Van Diemen's Land (Tasmania) and New Zealand and proving Australia was a distinct landmass rather than part of a vast southern continent connected to Antarctica. For over a century afterward, European interest in the region faded, and the outline of Australia's coasts remained incomplete, with its eastern side entirely uncharted by Europeans.

That changed with the voyages of British navigator James Cook, whose ship Endeavour reached the eastern coast of Australia in 1770. Cook charted the coastline from Point Hicks to Cape York, landed at Botany Bay, and claimed the eastern seaboard for Britain as New South Wales. His three Pacific voyages between 1768 and 1779 also mapped New Zealand in detail and charted numerous island groups across the Pacific, transforming European knowledge of the ocean and its peoples.

Cook's charts made possible Britain's decision to found a penal colony at Botany Bay; the First Fleet arrived in January 1788, landing at Sydney Cove and beginning permanent European settlement of Australia. The era of pure exploration gave way to colonization, irrevocably reshaping the lives of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples who had inhabited the continent for tens of thousands of years.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Thirty Years' War gave way to the Enlightenment, and Britain and France built rival global empires.
  • Asia: the Qing dynasty consolidated its rule over China as the Mughal Empire declined in India.
  • North America: thirteen British colonies grew, rebelled, and in 1776 declared independence as the United States.
