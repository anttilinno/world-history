Tiwanaku

South America · 550 CE – 1000 CE

On the windswept altiplano near the southern shore of Lake Titicaca, in what is now western Bolivia, a ceremonial center grew from a modest village into one of the ancient Andes' great capitals. By around 550 CE, Tiwanaku had emerged as the seat of a highland state that would dominate the region for the next four centuries, drawing on the lake's fisheries and a distinctive raised-field agricultural system, known as suka kollus, that let farmers grow potatoes and other crops at an altitude above 3,800 meters where frost was a constant threat.

Tiwanaku's rulers expressed their power through monumental stone architecture: the Akapana platform mound, the semi-subterranean Sunken Temple, and the Kalasasaya enclosure with its famed Gateway of the Sun, carved from a single block of andesite and decorated with a staff-bearing deity thought to be an early form of the god later known to the Inca as Viracocha. Massive andesite and sandstone blocks, some transported many kilometers from quarries and across the lake, were fitted with copper cramps and precise joints, evidence of sophisticated engineering and organized labor. At its height, around 800 to 1000 CE, the city may have housed tens of thousands of people and anchored a broader cultural and economic sphere that extended into present-day Peru and Chile, sustained by llama caravans trading textiles, obsidian, and other goods across the highlands.

Tiwanaku's influence spread less through outright military conquest than through religious prestige, trade networks, and colonization of resource-producing regions, contrasting with its contemporary and sometime rival, the Wari state to the northwest. Around 1000 CE, a prolonged drought disrupted the lake-fed agriculture that had sustained the city's dense population, and Tiwanaku collapsed as a political center, its population dispersing into smaller communities. Its legacy endured, however: later Andean peoples, including the Inca, regarded Tiwanaku's ruins as a sacred place of origin and incorporated its imagery and architectural ideas into their own imperial traditions.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Byzantine Empire and the rising Carolingian dynasty were reshaping the post-Roman world.
  • Asia: China's Tang dynasty flourished as a cosmopolitan power, while Islamic caliphates spread across the Middle East.
  • North America: Ancestral Puebloan communities were building great houses at Chaco Canyon.
