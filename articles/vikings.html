Vikings

Europe · 793 CE – 1066 CE

The Viking Age is conventionally dated from 793 CE, when Norse raiders sacked the monastery at Lindisfarne on England's northeast coast, sending shockwaves through Christian Europe. Drawing on shallow-draft longships that could cross open ocean or slip up rivers, seafarers from Denmark, Norway, and Sweden began a two-and-a-half-century era of raiding, trading, and settlement that reached far beyond Scandinavia's fjords and forests.

Norse expansion followed several directions at once. Danes and Norwegians raided and later settled in the British Isles, founding towns like Dublin and carving out the Danelaw in England, while others pushed into Normandy, which took its name from these "Northmen." Swedish Vikings, known as the Rus, traveled the river systems of Eastern Europe to trade with and raid Byzantium and the Islamic Abbasid Caliphate, reaching Constantinople and Baghdad and helping found the political entities that became Kievan Rus. To the west, Norse explorers colonized Iceland and Greenland, and around 1000 CE Leif Erikson's expedition reached the coast of Newfoundland, making the Vikings the first Europeans known to have set foot in the Americas.

Viking society was not merely predatory: it built extensive trade networks moving furs, amber, walrus ivory, and slaves in exchange for silver, silk, and spices, and its rune-carved stones, sagas, and shipbuilding remain landmark cultural achievements. Scandinavian kings gradually consolidated power and converted to Christianity, folding their realms into the mainstream of medieval European monarchy. The age is usually said to close in 1066, when Norway's King Harald Hardrada fell at the Battle of Stamford Bridge and, weeks later, the Norman descendants of earlier Viking settlers conquered England at Hastings.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Tang dynasty gave way to the Song in China, while the Abbasid Caliphate's Islamic Golden Age flourished in Baghdad.
  • Africa: the Empire of Ghana grew wealthy controlling trans-Saharan gold and salt trade routes.
  • North America: the Ancestral Puebloans were building the great houses of Chaco Canyon, and Mississippian mound-builders were rising at Cahokia.
