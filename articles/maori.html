Māori New Zealand

Oceania · 1300 – present

Around 1300 CE, Polynesian voyagers navigating by stars, currents, and bird flight reached Aotearoa — New Zealand — completing the last major landmass settlement in human history. Sailing east from central Eastern Polynesia in large double-hulled canoes, they found a temperate, forested archipelago far larger and colder than the tropical islands they had left, with no other humans and no land mammals besides bats. Over generations, the settlers adapted their tropical horticultural traditions to a new climate, developing distinct Māori language, carving, weaving, and oral tradition while organizing into iwi (tribes) and hapū (sub-tribes) tracing descent from named ancestral canoes.

Isolated from the rest of the world for roughly five centuries, Māori society diversified across the islands. Early settlers hunted the giant flightless moa to extinction within a few centuries, prompting a shift toward horticulture — cultivating kumara (sweet potato) and other crops brought from Polynesia — and intensive fishing and shellfish gathering. Fortified hilltop settlements called pā, sophisticated waka (canoes), and an intricate system of whakapapa (genealogy) and tikanga (custom) marked a flourishing, warlike, and artistically rich culture, expressed in moko (tattooing), whakairo (carving), and a rich mythology of gods and ancestors.

Sustained contact with the outside world began with Dutch explorer Abel Tasman's 1642 sighting and British explorer James Cook's 1769 landfall, followed by whalers, missionaries, and traders. The 1840 Treaty of Waitangi between the British Crown and Māori chiefs nominally guaranteed Māori land and authority, but was followed by mass European settlement, land confiscations, and the New Zealand Wars of the 1860s, which devastated Māori population and landholding. Māori language and culture declined sharply through the twentieth century under assimilationist policy.

From the 1970s, a Māori cultural and political renaissance — including the Waitangi Tribunal, land rights activism, and language-revival kōhanga reo (language nests) — reversed much of this decline. Today Māori remain a significant and growing part of New Zealand's population, with te reo Māori an official language and Māori culture, from haka to art to politics, central to the nation's identity.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Renaissance was dawning in Italy as the first waka reached Aotearoa's shores.
  • Asia: the Ming dynasty was consolidating power in China and would soon send Zheng He's treasure fleets across the Indian Ocean.
  • North America: the Aztec Empire was rising in the Valley of Mexico, centuries before European contact reached either shore.
