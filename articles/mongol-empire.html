Mongol Empire

Asia · 1206 – 1368

In 1206, a tribal chieftain named Temujin united the fractious steppe peoples of Mongolia and was proclaimed Genghis Khan at a kurultai on the Onon River. Welding together highly mobile horse archers, a strict meritocratic command structure, and a talent for absorbing conquered administrators and technologies, he launched campaigns that shattered the Khwarazmian Empire, subdued northern China, and pushed Mongol power from the Pacific to the Caspian within two decades.

Under his successors the empire grew larger still, eventually becoming the largest contiguous land empire in history, stretching from Korea and China to Eastern Europe and the Middle East. Ogedei Khan absorbed the Jin dynasty and sent armies into Russia and Poland; Mongke and Kublai Khan extended conquests into Persia, Baghdad, and southern China, where Kublai founded the Yuan dynasty in 1271. The empire eventually split into four khanates — the Yuan in China, the Ilkhanate in Persia, the Golden Horde in Russia, and the Chagatai Khanate in Central Asia — loosely linked by shared Chinggisid descent.

Mongol rule, however brutal its conquests, knit Eurasia together as never before. The Pax Mongolica secured the Silk Road, letting merchants, missionaries, and travelers such as Marco Polo cross the continent, while paper money, printing, gunpowder weapons, and administrative practices diffused between China, the Islamic world, and Europe. That same connectivity carried the Black Death westward from Central Asia in the 1340s, killing tens of millions.

Mongol power in China ebbed as the Yuan dynasty succumbed to famine, factional strife, and rebellion; in 1368 the Ming dynasty drove the Mongols back to the steppe, marking the empire's effective end even as Chinggisid khanates and successor states lingered in Central Asia and Russia for centuries.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Mongol invasions of Russia, Poland, and Hungary coincided with the height of Gothic cathedral building and the rise of Italian city-state banking.
  • Africa: the Mali Empire was rising under Sundiata Keita and would soon send Mansa Musa on his famous pilgrimage to Mecca.
  • North America: the Aztecs had not yet arrived in the Valley of Mexico, while the Ancestral Puebloans were abandoning Chaco Canyon and Mesa Verde.
