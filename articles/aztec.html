Aztec Empire

North America · 1325 – 1521

According to their own tradition, the Mexica people were wandering migrants from a homeland called Aztlán who settled in the Valley of Mexico after seeing a prophesied eagle perched on a cactus, devouring a serpent. In 1325 they founded Tenochtitlan on a swampy island in Lake Texcoco, building it up with chinampas — raised agricultural plots — and causeways linking it to the mainland. In 1428 the Mexica joined the neighboring city-states of Texcoco and Tlacopan in the Triple Alliance, overthrowing their former overlords and beginning a rapid expansion across central Mexico.

Over the following century the Triple Alliance, dominated by Tenochtitlan, extended tribute-based control from the Gulf Coast to the Pacific, without directly administering most conquered territory but instead demanding regular payments of goods, textiles, and captives for religious sacrifice. By the early 1500s Tenochtitlan had grown into one of the largest cities in the world, home to perhaps 200,000 people, with a great twin-temple pyramid, a bustling market at Tlatelolco, aqueducts, and a sophisticated calendar and priestly bureaucracy. Aztec society prized disciplined warriors, elaborate poetry, and a religion centered on maintaining cosmic order through ritual, including human sacrifice on a scale that shocked later European observers.

The empire's dependence on tribute and fear left many subject peoples resentful, a weakness the Spanish conquistador Hernán Cortés exploited after landing in 1519. Allying with the Tlaxcalans and other rivals of Tenochtitlan, Cortés marched on the capital, and although the Aztecs under Moctezuma II and later Cuauhtémoc resisted fiercely, a devastating smallpox epidemic — introduced by the Europeans — killed huge numbers of the population, including much of its leadership. Tenochtitlan fell after a prolonged siege in 1521, and the Spanish built Mexico City atop its ruins, ending Aztec political independence but leaving a lasting imprint on Mexican language, cuisine, and identity.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Renaissance was reshaping art and thought, and the printing press had just spread across the continent.
  • Asia: the Ming dynasty ruled China, and the Ottoman Empire was expanding after taking Constantinople in 1453.
  • Africa: the Songhai Empire dominated the western Sahel, controlling trade through Timbuktu.
