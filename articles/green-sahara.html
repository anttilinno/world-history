Green Sahara pastoralists

Africa · 10000 BCE – 3000 BCE

Around 10,000 BCE, a shift in Earth's orbital geometry strengthened the West African monsoon, pushing rain far north into what is today the Sahara Desert. Grasslands, seasonal rivers, and shallow lakes spread across a region now defined by dunes, and the area entered the phase geologists call the African Humid Period — popularly the "Green Sahara." Early inhabitants were hunter-fisher-gatherers, drawn to a landscape of savanna and wetland dotted with hippos, crocodiles, and fish.

By the sixth and fifth millennia BCE, some of these communities had adopted cattle herding, among the earliest pastoral economies in Africa, later supplemented by sheep and goats introduced from Southwest Asia. Herders moved seasonally between pastures and permanent water sources, leaving behind stone cattle burials, ceremonial megaliths at sites like Nabta Playa in southern Egypt, and thousands of rock paintings and engravings across the Tassili n'Ajjer and Acacus mountains depicting cattle, herders, dancers, and daily life in a landscape unrecognizable as desert.

The humid period was never uniform: rainfall weakened and fluctuated over centuries, and lakes such as the vast Lake Mega-Chad expanded and contracted. From roughly 5000 BCE the monsoon began a long retreat, and by about 3000 BCE the drying became irreversible, fragmenting the green Sahara into isolated refuges and then the world's largest hot desert.

As pastures vanished, populations funneled toward remaining water — the Nile valley, the Chad basin, and oases — carrying cattle culture, herding knowledge, and possibly linguistic and cultural roots that fed into the rise of Nile Valley societies, including early Egypt. The rock art left across the now-hyperarid mountains remains the most vivid record of a Sahara that was once green.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: farming communities in the Fertile Crescent domesticated wheat, barley, sheep, and goats, building early towns like Jericho and Çatalhöyük.
  • Europe: postglacial hunter-gatherers repopulated the continent, with farming arriving from the Near East only toward the end of this span.
  • North America: Paleoindian and early Archaic peoples, including Clovis hunters, spread across the continent as the last ice age ended.
