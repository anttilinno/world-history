Ice Age hunter-gatherers

Europe · 40000 BCE – 10000 BCE

By around 40,000 BCE, anatomically modern humans had spread across Europe, overlapping for several thousand years with the continent's resident Neanderthals before the latter disappeared from the archaeological record. This period, the Upper Paleolithic, is marked by a sharp increase in symbolic and technical sophistication: finely worked blade tools, sewn clothing, bone needles, and the earliest known figurative art, including the ivory carvings of the Swabian Jura and the female figurines known as Venus statuettes.

Small bands of hunter-gatherers followed herds of reindeer, horse, bison, and woolly mammoth across a landscape of steppe and tundra shaped by glacial cycles. They sheltered in caves, rock overhangs, and mammoth-bone dwellings on the open plains, and left behind a remarkable record of painted and engraved caves — Chauvet, decorated as early as 36,000 BCE, and Lascaux, painted around 17,000 BCE — whose vivid depictions of horses, aurochs, lions, and mammoths remain among the most celebrated achievements of prehistoric art.

The Last Glacial Maximum, around 21,000 BCE, pushed ice sheets deep into northern Europe and forced populations into southern refuges in Iberia, Italy, and the Balkans, from which the continent was later repopulated. As the climate warmed after about 15,000 BCE, groups spread north again, developing regional toolkit traditions such as the Magdalenian.

By 10,000 BCE, the retreat of the ice and the onset of the Holocene were transforming the landscape, opening the way for forests to replace tundra and for the herds that Ice Age hunters depended on to shrink or shift range. Within a few thousand years, farming communities spreading from the Near East would begin to replace this long foraging way of life, but the cave art and craftsmanship of Ice Age Europe remain foundational evidence for the origins of human symbolic culture.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: modern humans continued to diversify in their ancestral homeland, developing regional stone-tool traditions and early symbolic ornaments.
  • Oceania: seafaring peoples had already crossed open water to reach and settle Australia and New Guinea by around 50,000 BCE.
  • Asia: humans pushed into Siberia's harsh interior, laying the groundwork for the later migrations that would carry people across the Bering land bridge into the Americas.
