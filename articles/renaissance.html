Renaissance

Europe · 1400 – 1600

The Renaissance began in the city-states of northern Italy — Florence chief among them — where wealthy merchant families like the Medici funded artists and scholars who looked back to Greek and Roman antiquity for inspiration. Fueled by trade wealth, the rediscovery of classical texts (many preserved by Byzantine and Islamic scholars), and a new confidence in human reason and creativity, the movement marked a deliberate break from medieval tradition, giving the era its name: a "rebirth" of classical learning.

Its defining achievements spanned art, science, and thought. Painters and sculptors such as Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, and Raphael mastered linear perspective and anatomical realism, producing works like the Mona Lisa, the Sistine Chapel ceiling, and David. Architects revived classical forms in buildings like Florence's cathedral dome, engineered by Brunelleschi. Humanist scholars including Petrarch and Erasmus championed the study of classical languages and secular subjects alongside theology, while Machiavelli reshaped political thought. Johannes Gutenberg's printing press, developed around 1440, spread these ideas across Europe far faster than manuscripts ever could.

By the late 1400s and 1500s, the Renaissance had spread beyond Italy to France, the Low Countries, Germany, and England, blending with local traditions to produce figures like Albrecht Dürer and later Shakespeare. It overlapped with the Age of Exploration and the early Scientific Revolution, as thinkers like Copernicus began questioning inherited cosmology. The movement gradually gave way to the Reformation's religious upheavals and the more formal styles of the Baroque, but its legacy — in humanism, empirical inquiry, and artistic technique — became a foundation of the modern Western world.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Ming dynasty ruled China and sent Zheng He's treasure fleets across the Indian Ocean, while the Ottoman Empire captured Constantinople in 1453.
  • Africa: the Songhai Empire flourished along the Niger River, with Timbuktu a renowned center of Islamic scholarship.
  • North America: the Aztec Empire built its capital Tenochtitlan into one of the world's largest cities before Spanish conquest in 1521.
