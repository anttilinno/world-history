Sumer

Asia · 4500 BCE – 1900 BCE

In the marshy floodplain of southern Mesopotamia, between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, farming villages grew by around 4500 BCE into the world's first cities. Uruk, Eridu, Ur, Lagash, and Nippur developed monumental temple complexes, irrigation networks, and specialized labor, giving rise to a distinctly urban civilization the Sumerians called their land "the black-headed people's" home. By the fourth millennium BCE, Uruk may have held tens of thousands of residents, making it the largest settlement on Earth at the time.

Sumer's defining achievement was writing. Around 3200 BCE, temple administrators developed pictographic accounting marks on clay tablets that evolved into cuneiform, a wedge-shaped script pressed with a reed stylus. Cuneiform soon recorded far more than grain tallies: laws, myths such as the Epic of Gilgamesh, hymns, and letters. Sumerians also pioneered the wheel, the plow, bronze tools, the sexagesimal number system that still gives us 60-minute hours and 360-degree circles, and some of the earliest codified law codes.

Politically, Sumer was never a single unified state but a shifting patchwork of independent city-states, each ruled by a king under the patronage of a local god, frequently warring over land and water rights. Around 2334 BCE, Sargon of Akkad conquered the Sumerian cities and built the first known multi-ethnic empire, and Sumerian language and culture persisted under Akkadian rule and its brief Ur III revival (c. 2112–2004 BCE). By 1900 BCE, waves of Amorite migration, Elamite incursions, and the gradual eclipse of Sumerian by Akkadian as a spoken language folded Sumer's political identity into the broader Mesopotamian world, though its script, myths, and legal traditions shaped Babylonian and Assyrian civilization for two more millennia.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Africa: Egypt's Nile Valley communities were still generations away from unification under the first pharaohs.
  • Europe: farming communities were raising the first megalithic monuments in Atlantic Europe.
  • South America: small hunter-gatherer and early agricultural groups occupied the Andes and coast, well before the rise of Norte Chico's cities.
