Achaemenid Persia

Asia · 550 BCE – 330 BCE

Around 550 BCE, Cyrus the Great overthrew his Median overlords and began welding the Iranian plateau into a new power. Within two decades he had conquered Lydia in Anatolia and the great city of Babylon, famously releasing the Jews from their exile and permitting subject peoples to keep their own laws, languages, and gods — a policy of tolerant rule that set the Achaemenid Empire apart from earlier conquerors like Assyria.

Cyrus's successors pushed the empire to its greatest extent. Cambyses II added Egypt, and Darius I, who seized the throne after a succession crisis, organized the realm into provinces called satrapies linked by the Royal Road and a relay postal system, standardized coinage and law, and built the ceremonial capital of Persepolis. At its height the empire stretched from the Indus Valley to Thrace and Libya, ruling perhaps 40 percent of the world's population — the largest empire yet seen.

Darius and his son Xerxes I tried to extend Persian power into mainland Greece but were checked at Marathon, Salamis, and Plataea, a costly setback that nonetheless left the empire's core intact for another century and a half. Persia remained the dominant Near Eastern power, its administration and infrastructure influencing later empires, until internal strife and a weakened military left it vulnerable.

In 334 BCE, Alexander the Great invaded from Macedon, and after decisive victories at Issus and Gaugamela he took Persepolis and burned its palace. The last Achaemenid king, Darius III, was murdered by his own satrap in 330 BCE, ending Persian rule and opening the Hellenistic age, though Persian administrative and cultural traditions endured under Alexander's successors and later Iranian dynasties.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: Athens and Sparta were forging classical Greek civilization, and Rome was still a small republic contending with its Italian neighbors.
  • Africa: Nubia's Kingdom of Kush ruled from Meroë, and Carthage dominated Mediterranean trade from North Africa.
  • Asia: China's Warring States period saw rival kingdoms battle toward eventual unification, while the Maurya Empire was rising in India soon after Persia's fall.
