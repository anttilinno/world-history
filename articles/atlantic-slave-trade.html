Atlantic slave trade

Africa · 1500 – 1870

Following Portuguese and Spanish voyages to West Africa and the Americas in the late 1400s, European traders began forcibly shipping enslaved Africans across the Atlantic to supply labor for colonial plantations and mines. The trade grew from a small trickle in the early 1500s into a vast, systematized commerce run by Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch, French, and above all British merchants, who purchased captives from African coastal kingdoms and middlemen in exchange for textiles, firearms, and other goods — a system historians call the triangular trade linking Europe, Africa, and the Americas.

At its height in the 1700s, hundreds of ships a year crossed the Middle Passage, packing captives into holds under conditions that killed roughly one in eight during the voyage alone. Brazil and the Caribbean sugar colonies absorbed the largest numbers, while smaller streams fed the tobacco and rice plantations of British North America. Enslaved Africans and their descendants built much of the wealth of Atlantic port cities and plantation economies, while enduring lifelong bondage enforced by law and violence; their labor, resistance, and culture reshaped the societies of the Americas, from language and cuisine to music and religion.

Resistance was constant, from shipboard revolts to marronage and uprisings such as the Haitian Revolution of 1791–1804, which ended slavery in Saint-Domingue and inspired abolitionist movements elsewhere. Britain outlawed the trade in 1807 and used its navy to suppress it, the United States banned the trade in 1808, and one by one other nations followed, though slavery itself persisted decades longer — in the U.S. until 1865, in Brazil until 1888. The last documented slave ship crossed to Cuba around 1867, closing a trade that had forcibly transported an estimated 12.5 million Africans, with lasting demographic, economic, and social consequences across three continents.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Renaissance gave way to the Enlightenment, the Reformation, and the Industrial Revolution.
  • Asia: the Ottoman, Mughal, and Qing empires flourished before facing growing European pressure.
  • North America: European colonization displaced Indigenous nations as the United States formed and expanded westward.
