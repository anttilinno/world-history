Tokugawa Japan

Asia · 1603 – 1868

In 1603, Tokugawa Ieyasu, victor of the decisive Battle of Sekigahara three years earlier, was named shogun by the emperor and established a military government in Edo (modern Tokyo). This ended over a century of civil war among rival warlords and inaugurated the Edo period, a long era of centralized feudal rule that would hold Japan together under the Tokugawa house for more than 250 years.

The shogunate imposed strict social order through a rigid four-tier class system topped by samurai, required regional lords (daimyo) to alternate residence between their domains and Edo under the sankin-kotai system to keep them financially weak and politically loyal, and by the 1630s had all but sealed the country off from the outside world. Under this policy of sakoku, foreign trade and contact were confined almost entirely to a Dutch and Chinese trading post at Nagasaki, while Christianity was suppressed and Japanese subjects were forbidden to travel abroad. Within these closed borders, Japan enjoyed remarkable internal peace, urban growth, rising literacy, and a flourishing merchant culture that produced kabuki theater, ukiyo-e woodblock prints, and haiku poetry.

By the early nineteenth century, a money economy had eroded the samurai class's fixed rice stipends, peasant uprisings grew more frequent, and Western powers pressed for access. Commodore Matthew Perry's American naval squadron forced open Japanese ports in 1853–1854, exposing the shogunate's inability to resist foreign demands and triggering a crisis of legitimacy. Discontented domains toppled the shogunate in the Meiji Restoration of 1868, ending samurai rule and launching Japan's rapid modernization.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Thirty Years' War, the Scientific Revolution, and later the French Revolution and Napoleonic Wars reshaped the continent.
  • North America: English colonists founded Jamestown and Plymouth, and the United States won independence and expanded westward.
  • Asia: the Qing dynasty rose in China, and the Mughal Empire flourished and then fragmented under British pressure in India.
