Hopewell tradition

North America · 100 BCE – 500 CE

The Hopewell tradition was not a single society but a broad network of related cultures across eastern North America, named after a farm in Ohio where earthworks were first excavated in the 1890s. Emerging from earlier Adena mound-building practices, Hopewell peoples lived in dispersed hamlets along river valleys, sustained by hunting, gathering, and small-scale cultivation of native plants such as goosefoot, sunflower, and squash, well before maize became a dietary staple.

What defined the Hopewell world was not political unity but the Hopewell Interaction Sphere, a vast web of exchange that moved obsidian from the Rocky Mountains, copper from the Great Lakes, mica from the southern Appalachians, marine shells from the Gulf Coast, and shark teeth from Florida into the Ohio and Illinois river valleys. There, communities transformed these materials into finely worked ornaments, effigy pipes, and ceremonial tools, many of which were buried with the dead in elaborate mortuary rituals. This exchange network sustained not just trade but shared religious and artistic ideas across thousands of miles.

Hopewell peoples are best remembered for monumental earthworks: geometric enclosures of circles, squares, and octagons, and effigy and burial mounds built with astonishing precision, some aligned to lunar and solar cycles. Sites such as Newark Earthworks and the mounds of Chillicothe in Ohio represent an engineering and organizational achievement comparable to contemporary monument-building elsewhere in the world, accomplished without draft animals, metal tools, or a centralized state.

By around 400–500 CE, the long-distance exchange network contracted and large-scale earthwork construction declined, for reasons still debated among archaeologists, possibly including climatic shifts, population dispersal, or changing social and religious practices. The Hopewell tradition nonetheless left a lasting legacy, shaping the later Mississippian mound-building cultures that flourished in the same region centuries afterward.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Roman Republic gave way to the Roman Empire, which expanded to its greatest extent before beginning its slow decline.
  • Asia: the Han dynasty ruled China, and the Silk Road linked Chinese markets to Rome and Central Asia.
  • South America: the Moche civilization was rising on the northern coast of Peru, building its own monumental adobe pyramids.
