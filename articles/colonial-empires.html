European colonial empires

Europe · 1500 – 1914

Portuguese and Spanish voyages of the late 1400s opened a new age of overseas expansion. Portugal seized coastal trading posts around Africa, the Indian Ocean, and Brazil, while Spain's conquest of the Aztec and Inca empires delivered vast territories and silver wealth in the Americas. The 1494 Treaty of Tordesillas split much of the unexplored world between the two Iberian crowns, and by the mid-1500s galleons were carrying silver, spices, and enslaved Africans across three oceans in a genuinely global system of exchange.

From the early 1600s, the Dutch, English, and French challenged Iberian dominance. Chartered companies — the Dutch and English East India Companies foremost among them — combined trade with private armies and administration, seizing footholds in the Caribbean, North America, West Africa, and Asia. The Atlantic slave trade forcibly transported over twelve million Africans to plantation colonies, fueling sugar, tobacco, and cotton economies that enriched European metropoles. Rivalry among these powers repeatedly spilled into global warfare, from the Anglo-Dutch wars to the Seven Years' War, which left Britain dominant in North America and India.

The loss of the Thirteen Colonies in 1783 and the collapse of Spanish and Portuguese rule in Latin America by the 1820s ended the first wave of empire, but a second, more intensive wave followed. Industrialization gave Europe overwhelming military and economic advantage, and the late 1800s "Scramble for Africa," formalized at the 1884–85 Berlin Conference, divided nearly the entire continent among European powers within a generation. By 1914 Britain, France, the Netherlands, Portugal, and Spain, joined by newer entrants like Germany and Belgium, ruled territories spanning Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and the Pacific.

These empires reshaped global borders, languages, religions, and economies, extracting resources and labor while imposing administrative and legal structures that outlasted colonial rule itself. The First World War strained the European powers and emboldened independence movements, beginning a slow retreat that accelerated after 1945; by the late twentieth century almost all colonies had become sovereign states, though the political, economic, and cultural legacies of empire remain deeply embedded worldwide.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Ming and Qing dynasties in China and the Mughal Empire in India remained wealthier and more populous than any European state for much of this period.
  • Africa: the Ashanti, Benin, and Ethiopian kingdoms, among others, resisted or negotiated with European traders and armies before eventual conquest.
  • North America: the United States achieved independence in 1776 and expanded westward, itself displacing Indigenous nations across the continent.
