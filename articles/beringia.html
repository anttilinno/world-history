Crossing from Beringia

North America · 20000 BCE – 10000 BCE

During the last glacial maximum, sea levels fell far enough to expose Beringia, a vast tundra plain connecting Siberia to Alaska where the shallow Bering Strait now lies. Small bands of hunter-gatherers, already adapted to Ice Age Siberia, moved into this land bridge, following mammoth, bison, and other megafauna. Genetic and archaeological evidence suggests these populations lingered in Beringia for thousands of years — a "Beringian standstill" — before glacial retreat opened routes further south.

Exactly how and when people first pushed beyond Beringia remains debated. Long-favored models pointed to an ice-free corridor opening between the retreating Laurentide and Cordilleran ice sheets around 13,000 BCE, but growing evidence — including sites like Monte Verde in Chile dated to roughly 14,500 years ago — suggests earlier movement along the Pacific coast, using boats and following a rich shoreline of kelp, fish, and shellfish southward before the interior corridor was passable.

By around 11,000 BCE, descendants of these migrants had spread widely across North America, leaving distinctive fluted stone points associated with the Clovis culture, among the first well-documented toolmaking traditions on the continent. From this initial population, people continued southward with remarkable speed, reaching the southern tip of South America within a few thousand years.

This migration completed the human settlement of the habitable world, leaving only Antarctica and remote Pacific islands unpeopled. It seeded the enormous diversity of later American cultures — from Arctic hunters to Mesoamerican and Andean civilizations — all descended, according to genetic evidence, from this same relatively small founding population that crossed from Siberia.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: Ice Age hunter-gatherers were painting the cave art of Lascaux and Altamira as glaciers retreated.
  • Asia: communities in the Levant and East Asia were beginning to experiment with early pottery and intensive foraging ahead of agriculture.
  • Africa: modern humans had long been established across the continent, developing regionally varied Later Stone Age toolkits.
