Medieval kingdoms

Europe · 500 CE – 1400 CE

After the collapse of the Western Roman Empire in the late 5th century, Europe fragmented into a patchwork of Germanic successor kingdoms — Franks, Visigoths, Ostrogoths, and Anglo-Saxons — that blended Roman administrative habits with tribal custom. The Christian Church, especially the papacy and a growing network of monasteries, became the main institution preserving literacy, learning, and a shared cultural identity across the former empire, copying classical texts and evangelizing pagan peoples from Ireland to Scandinavia.

Charlemagne's Frankish empire briefly reunified much of Western Europe around 800 CE, sparking a modest revival of learning before splintering under his successors and the pressure of Viking, Magyar, and Muslim raids. Out of that instability emerged feudalism: a decentralized order of kings, lords, knights, and peasants bound by mutual obligations of land and military service. From roughly 1000 CE, a warmer climate, better agricultural technology, and relative peace fueled population growth, the revival of towns and trade, and the rise of powerful monarchies in France, England, and the Iberian kingdoms.

The High Middle Ages (c. 1000–1300) were Europe's great age of building and thought: soaring Gothic cathedrals like Chartres and Notre-Dame, the founding of universities at Paris, Oxford, and Bologna, and the Crusades, which drew Europe into sustained contact — hostile and commercial alike — with the Byzantine and Islamic worlds. Chivalric knighthood, scholastic philosophy, and vernacular literature flourished alongside a thriving urban merchant class and craft guilds.

This expansion ended abruptly in the 14th century. The Great Famine of 1315–1317 and, far more devastatingly, the Black Death of 1347–1351 killed perhaps a third of Europe's population, shattering the manorial economy and triggering peasant revolts and labor shortages that reshaped rural society. Combined with the protracted Hundred Years' War between England and France, these crises closed the medieval order and set the stage for the Renaissance and the rise of centralized nation-states.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Asia: the Tang and Song dynasties made China the world's most advanced economy, while the Islamic Caliphates built a golden age of science and trade stretching from Spain to Central Asia.
  • Africa: the empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai grew rich on trans-Saharan gold and salt trade, and Great Zimbabwe rose in the south.
  • North America: the Mississippian culture built the mound city of Cahokia, while the Aztecs were founding Tenochtitlan by the 14th century.
