Wari Empire

South America · 600 CE – 1000 CE

Around 600 CE, a state centered on the city of Wari, near modern Ayacucho in the Peruvian highlands, began expanding outward from its Andean heartland. Drawing on the earlier religious and artistic traditions of Tiwanaku and Huarpa, the Wari built one of the first true empires in the Andes, imposing centralized administration over a diverse patchwork of coastal and highland peoples centuries before the Inca rose to power in the same region.

At its height, Wari influence stretched from near Cajamarca in the north to Cuzco and beyond in the south, administered through provincial centers such as Pikillacta and Viracochapampa that were built to standardized architectural plans. The Wari are best known for engineering a vast network of stone-paved roads later absorbed into the Inca road system, along with terraced agriculture, canal irrigation, and storage complexes that allowed the state to move grain and labor across dramatically different ecological zones. They also produced fine polychrome ceramics and tapestries whose imagery, including a staff-bearing deity, spread widely across the region as a marker of Wari authority or affiliation.

Wari control depended heavily on direct administration backed by military garrisons, a costlier model than the Inca's later mix of conquest and negotiated alliance. By around 1000 CE, the empire had fragmented amid drought, resource strain, and the weakening of central authority, and its cities were largely abandoned. Even so, the roads, terraces, and administrative templates the Wari left behind gave the Inca a ready-made foundation to build upon some four centuries later.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Carolingian dynasty was consolidating power, with Charlemagne crowned emperor in 800 CE.
  • Asia: Tang dynasty China flourished as a cosmopolitan power, while the Islamic Abbasid Caliphate rose in the Middle East.
  • North America: Ancestral Puebloans were building the great houses of Chaco Canyon, and Maya city-states neared their Classic-period peak.
