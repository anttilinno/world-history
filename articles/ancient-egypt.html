Ancient Egypt

Africa · 3100 BCE – 30 BCE

Around 3100 BCE, the kingdoms of Upper and Lower Egypt were unified under a single ruler — traditionally Narmer — creating one of the world's first territorial states. Bound together by the Nile, whose annual flood fertilized the fields with remarkable reliability, Egypt developed a centralized monarchy, a hieroglyphic writing system, and a religious worldview centered on divine kingship and the afterlife.

The Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2181 BCE) was the age of the pyramids: the Step Pyramid of Djoser and the Great Pyramid of Giza, built for Khufu around 2560 BCE, remain among the largest structures ever raised by preindustrial societies. After periods of fragmentation, the Middle and New Kingdoms saw Egypt expand into Nubia and the Levant, with rulers such as Hatshepsut, Thutmose III, Akhenaten, Tutankhamun, and Ramesses II presiding over an empire of temples, diplomacy, and monumental art.

From about 1200 BCE, Egypt weathered the Bronze Age collapse better than most of its neighbors, but slowly declined under Libyan, Nubian, Assyrian, and Persian rulers. Alexander the Great's conquest in 332 BCE brought the Greek-speaking Ptolemaic dynasty, which ended when Cleopatra VII was defeated and Rome annexed Egypt in 30 BCE — closing a three-thousand-year run as one of history's most durable civilizations.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: megalith builders were still assembling Stonehenge as the Giza pyramids rose.
  • Asia: Sumer's cities were flourishing, and the Indus Valley civilization was at its height.
  • South America: Norte Chico (Caral) was building pyramids of its own on the Peruvian coast.
