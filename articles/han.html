Han dynasty

Asia · 206 BCE – 220 CE

The Han dynasty rose from the collapse of the short-lived Qin, when the commoner-turned-general Liu Bang defeated his rivals and founded a new imperial house in 206 BCE. Han rulers kept the Qin's centralized bureaucracy and standardized administration but tempered its harsh Legalism with Confucian ideals, creating a model of governance — an emperor advised by scholar-officials selected partly on merit — that would define Chinese statecraft for two millennia.

Under Emperor Wu (r. 141–87 BCE), Han China reached its height: armies pushed deep into Central Asia and Korea, Confucianism became state orthodoxy taught at an imperial academy, and merchants, monks, and envoys carried silk, paper, and ideas along trade routes that later generations would call the Silk Road. Han craftsmen and scholars produced the first true paper, advances in cast iron and steel, seismographs, and a rigorous historical tradition exemplified by Sima Qian's Records of the Grand Historian, while the civil service examination system began to take shape as a path to office based on learning rather than birth alone.

A brief interruption came in 9 CE, when the reformer Wang Mang usurped the throne and founded the short-lived Xin dynasty before the Han was restored in 25 CE with its capital moved east to Luoyang. This Later Han enjoyed renewed prosperity and continued scholarship, but eunuch factionalism, land concentration among great families, and the Yellow Turban peasant rebellion of 184 CE eroded central authority. The dynasty formally ended in 220 CE when the last emperor abdicated, splitting China into the Three Kingdoms.

The Han left a legacy so deep that China's majority ethnic group and its script still bear its name. Its bureaucratic model, Confucian ideology, and role in opening exchange between East and West made it the classical anchor against which later Chinese dynasties, and their own claims to unity and legitimacy, would be measured.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Roman Republic gave way to the Roman Empire, reaching its own imperial height under Augustus and his successors.
  • Africa: the Kingdom of Kush ruled from Meroë, and Roman Egypt anchored Mediterranean trade with the Red Sea and India.
  • North America: Hopewell mound-building cultures flourished across the eastern woodlands, exchanging goods across a wide trade network.
