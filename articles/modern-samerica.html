Modern republics

South America · 1830 – present

By 1830, the wars of independence led by Simón Bolívar and José de San Martín had broken Spanish and Portuguese rule across South America, but Bolívar's dream of a unified Gran Colombia collapsed that same year into Venezuela, Ecuador, and New Granada (later Colombia). The continent settled into a patchwork of fragile republics, ruled through much of the nineteenth century by regional caudillos and export-oriented elites who tied national economies to a single commodity — silver, guano, rubber, coffee, or beef — sold to industrializing Europe and North America.

The early twentieth century brought waves of European immigration, urbanization, and populist politics, epitomized by Argentina's Juan Perón and Brazil's Getúlio Vargas, who built mass movements around labor rights and industrial nationalism. Cold War tensions then pulled the region into a cycle of military coups and dictatorships, from Brazil's 1964 takeover to Chile's Augusto Pinochet and Argentina's "Dirty War" juntas, often backed by Washington against leftist and communist movements, most dramatically Fidel Castro's Cuban Revolution of 1959.

A "third wave" of democratization swept the continent from the 1980s, as military regimes gave way to elected civilian governments almost everywhere. Commodity booms, especially the China-driven surge of the 2000s, fueled a "pink tide" of left-leaning governments under leaders like Hugo Chávez, Lula da Silva, and Evo Morales, expanding social programs before falling commodity prices and corruption scandals brought renewed volatility and, in the 2010s–2020s, a swing back toward both populist and market-oriented politics.

Today South America is a continent of consolidated, if often turbulent, democracies grappling with inequality, deforestation of the Amazon, and dependence on commodity exports, even as it hosts major economies like Brazil and growing regional bodies such as Mercosur.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: industrialization, nationalism, and two world wars reshaped the continent before the Cold War split it in two.
  • Asia: the Qing dynasty fell, Japan industrialized and warred, and China and India emerged as postcolonial giants.
  • Africa: European colonial empires expanded across the continent, then dissolved into independent states after 1950.
