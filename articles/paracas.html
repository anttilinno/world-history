Paracas culture

South America · 800 BCE – 100 BCE

The Paracas culture emerged around 800 BCE on the arid southern coast of Peru, centered on the Paracas Peninsula and the Ica Valley. Named by the pioneering archaeologist Julio C. Tello, who excavated its cemeteries in the 1920s, Paracas grew out of earlier coastal traditions and developed into one of the most artistically accomplished societies of ancient South America, sustained by fishing, irrigation agriculture, and exchange with highland neighbors.

Paracas is best known for its textiles, widely regarded as among the finest ever produced by any pre-industrial society. Woven from cotton and camelid wool dyed in dozens of vivid colors, the elaborate embroidered mantles found wrapped around mummy bundles at sites like Cerro Colorado depict flying shamanic figures, felines, and trophy-head imagery with astonishing technical precision. The culture also practiced sophisticated cranial trepanation and skull deformation, and produced fine polychrome pottery, incising vessels before firing and adding resin-based pigments afterward — a technique distinct from that of later Nazca potters.

Paracas society buried its elite dead in deep, shaft-like necropolises, wrapping bodies in layer upon layer of textiles to form large funerary bundles; the exceptionally dry desert climate preserved these fabrics in vivid detail for over two thousand years. Around 100 BCE, Paracas traditions gave way to the Nazca culture, which inherited and further developed its artistic and ceramic techniques, particularly the famous Nazca lines, in the same region.
