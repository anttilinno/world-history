Maurya Empire

Asia · 322 BCE – 185 BCE

The Maurya Empire was founded around 322 BCE when Chandragupta Maurya, with the guidance of his advisor Chanakya (Kautilya), overthrew the Nanda dynasty of Magadha and began building the first empire to unify nearly the entire Indian subcontinent. Chandragupta expanded westward after Alexander the Great's death, defeating the Seleucid ruler Seleucus I Nicator around 305 BCE and absorbing large territories in the northwest in exchange for war elephants, establishing a state that stretched from Afghanistan to Bengal.

The empire reached its territorial and cultural peak under Ashoka the Great (r. c. 268–232 BCE), Chandragupta's grandson. Ashoka's brutal conquest of Kalinga around 261 BCE, with its massive casualties, is said to have filled him with remorse and led him to renounce further military expansion. He embraced Buddhism and promoted dhamma, a code of moral conduct emphasizing nonviolence, tolerance, and welfare, which he had inscribed on pillars and rocks across the empire — among the earliest deciphered written records in Indian history. Ashoka sent Buddhist missionaries as far as Sri Lanka and the Hellenistic world, helping transform Buddhism from a regional sect into a major world religion. The empire also featured a sophisticated administrative system, described in the Arthashastra, with a centralized bureaucracy, extensive road networks, and organized trade.

After Ashoka's death, the empire gradually weakened under less capable successors, strained by its sheer size, regional fragmentation, and financial pressures. The last Maurya emperor, Brihadratha, was assassinated in 185 BCE by his own military commander, Pushyamitra Shunga, who founded the Shunga dynasty. Although the empire lasted only about 140 years, it left a lasting legacy as a model of centralized governance and as the vehicle that carried Buddhism across Asia.

Meanwhile, elsewhere

  • Europe: the Roman Republic was consolidating control over the Italian peninsula and beginning the Punic Wars against Carthage.
  • Africa: Ptolemaic Egypt, founded by one of Alexander the Great's generals, was flourishing around Alexandria.
  • Asia: China's Warring States period was drawing to a close, culminating in unification under Qin Shi Huang in 221 BCE.
